# Lalibela — Ethiopia's Jerusalem

**Region:** Northern Ethiopia · **Best time:** October–June · **Recommended stay:** 2–3 nights

## Overview

In the 12th century, King Lalibela ordered the construction of a New Jerusalem in the Ethiopian highlands. What emerged from the red volcanic rock were eleven monolithic churches, carved downward into the earth, connected by tunnels and ceremonial passageways. Lalibela is not a ruin. It is a living pilgrimage site visited by thousands of white-robed Orthodox Christians every day.

No photograph prepares you for the moment you look down into the excavated courtyard and see Bet Giyorgis — the Church of St George — rising from the earth in the shape of a perfect Greek cross. It is one of the great sights of the world.

## Highlights

- **Bet Giyorgis** — the most photographed church in Ethiopia, carved as a perfect cross
- **Northern and Eastern church clusters** — interconnected by rock-hewn tunnels
- **Timkat (Epiphany)** — January 19th, the most spectacular Ethiopian Orthodox festival
- **Asheton Maryam monastery** — 45-minute mule ride above the town, panoramic views
- **Lalibela village** — the market, the bajaj rides, the injera and tej houses

## When to Visit

October to June is optimal. January brings Timkat — a three-day festival of enormous scale. Easter (Fasika) in April/May draws thousands of pilgrims. The rainy season (July–September) makes access harder but crowds are minimal and the landscape is green.

## Getting There

Daily flights from Addis Ababa (1 hour). Ethiopian Airlines operates the route. Road access from Gondar (approximately 8–10 hours on partly paved road) is an adventure in itself.

# Simien Mountains — The Roof of Africa

**Region:** Northern Ethiopia · **Best time:** October–March · **Recommended stay:** 3–7 nights trekking

## Overview

The Simien Mountains are the most dramatic highlands on the African continent. The main escarpment drops 1,500 metres in sheer cliff faces, revealing valleys that seem to belong to another planet. UNESCO listed the park in 1978 — one of Africa's first natural World Heritage Sites — for its extraordinary scenery and endemic wildlife.

This is the only place on earth to see gelada monkeys in large herds. At dawn, groups of 300–800 individuals emerge from the cliff edges where they sleep, moving across the Afroalpine moorland in a slow, grass-grazing mass. Nothing in Africa looks quite like this.

## Highlights

- **Gelada monkey herds** at Chenek and Sankaber — up to 800 in a single group
- **Ras Dashen** — Ethiopia's highest peak at 4,550m, multi-day summit trek
- **Walia ibex** — found only in the Simien Mountains, endemic and endangered
- **Lammergeier vultures** — dramatic bone-dropping behaviour visible from escarpment viewpoints
- **Cliff-edge campsites** — sleep at 3,600m with views across the escarpment

## Wildlife

The park is home to three endemic species found nowhere else:
- Gelada baboon (*Theropithecus gelada*) — 200,000+ population, herds of 300–800
- Walia ibex (*Capra walie*) — approximately 1,000 individuals remaining
- Ethiopian wolf (*Canis simensis*) — rare sightings in northern sections of park

## Trekking Routes

- **3-day loop:** Sankaber → Geech → Chenek → return (moderate)
- **5-day traverse:** Full escarpment traverse with Ras Dashen summit
- **7-day circuit:** Complete park circuit including remote northern sections

# Danakil Depression — The Most Alien Place on Earth

**Region:** Afar Region · **Best time:** November–February ONLY · **Recommended stay:** 3–4 nights

## Overview

The Danakil Depression sits 130 metres below sea level at the junction of three tectonic plates. It is one of the hottest places on earth — average annual temperature 34°C, peak temperatures exceeding 50°C in summer. The landscape looks like nothing else on this planet: sulphur springs painted in yellows and greens, active lava lakes glowing orange at night, vast salt flats stretching to the horizon.

Erta Ale — the "smoking mountain" in the Afar language — has held an active lava lake for over 100 years. It is one of only five permanent lava lakes in the world. Visiting at night, you walk across cooled lava to the crater rim and look down at liquid rock boiling 30 metres below.

This is not a comfortable journey. It requires physical fitness, heat tolerance, and a guide who knows the terrain. It is also one of the most extraordinary experiences available anywhere in the world.

## Highlights

- **Erta Ale lava lake** — night visit to the crater rim, active lava lake glowing in darkness
- **Dallol sulphur springs** — the most colourful landscape on earth, salt chimneys and acid pools
- **Salt caravans** — Afar nomads with camel trains extracting salt slabs from the flats
- **Afar villages** — one of the most remote and traditional cultures in Africa

## Practical Notes

- **Season:** November to February only. Summer travel is dangerous.
- **Fitness:** Moderate fitness required. Night trek to Erta Ale crater takes 2–3 hours each way.
- **Access:** Fly to Mekele, then 4x4 convoy across the desert.
- **Safety:** Sawla Tours uses established routes with Afar guides and armed escorts.

# Omo Valley — Where Ancient Cultures Survive

**Region:** Southern Ethiopia · **Best time:** September–January · **Recommended stay:** 4–7 nights

## Overview

The Omo Valley is one of the last places on earth where ancient cultures live entirely on their own terms. The river and its tributary valleys are home to approximately sixteen ethnic groups — the Mursi, Hamer, Karo, Benna, Tsemay, Dassanech, and others — each maintaining distinct languages, ritual practices, and aesthetic traditions that predate recorded history.

This is not a cultural show. These are living communities with complex social systems, cattle-based economies, and annual ritual cycles tied to the agricultural calendar. The Hamer bull-jumping ceremony — a young man's rite of passage — has been practised for centuries. The Mursi lip plate tradition is as culturally specific to this valley as anything you will encounter in the world.

## Highlights

- **Mursi people** — famous for clay lip plates, body paint, and ceremonial scarification
- **Hamer bull-jumping ceremony** — male initiation rite, cattle are lined up for the jump
- **Karo people** — famous for elaborate body paint and cliff paintings above the Omo River
- **Omo River** — canoe trips, hippos, crocodiles, river camp nights
- **Jinka and Key Afer markets** — extraordinary weekly gatherings of multiple tribes

## When to Go

September to January is optimal — dry season allows 4x4 access to remote areas. The bull-jumping ceremony season peaks October–November. Check with Sawla Tours specialists for ceremony timing when planning.

# Bale Mountains — Last Refuge of the Ethiopian Wolf

**Region:** Southern Ethiopia · **Best time:** October–March · **Recommended stay:** 3–5 nights

## Overview

The Bale Mountains National Park contains the largest remaining population of Ethiopian wolves — the world's rarest canid, found nowhere outside Ethiopia. The Sanetti Plateau at 4,000m is the highest point of the park and the best place on earth to see these animals in the wild. At dawn, wolves emerge from their burrows and begin the day's hunting — giant mole rats are their primary prey on the plateau.

The park also holds extraordinary botanical diversity: giant lobelia plants up to 5 metres tall, Afroalpine heather, and the Harenna forest on the southern slopes — one of the last remaining montane forests in Africa, home to black-and-white colobus monkeys and African wild dogs.

## Highlights

- **Ethiopian wolf** (*Canis simensis*) — the Web Valley and Sanetti Plateau hold 300+ wolves
- **Mountain nyala** (*Tragelaphus buxtoni*) — large antelope found only in the Ethiopian highlands
- **Sanetti Plateau** — at 4,000m, the plateau is other-worldly Afroalpine moorland
- **Harenna forest** — dense cloud forest on the southern escarpment, colobus monkeys
- **Giant lobelia** — 5-metre tall plants found only at altitude in Ethiopia

# Gondar — Ethiopia's Camelot

**Region:** Northern Ethiopia · **Best time:** Year-round · **Recommended stay:** 1–2 nights

## Overview

From 1636 to 1864, Gondar was the capital of the Ethiopian empire. Six successive emperors each built their own castle within the Royal Enclosure — a walled compound of castles, banquet halls, and ceremonial buildings that earned Gondar the nickname "the Camelot of Africa." The Enclosure is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most remarkable historical complexes in sub-Saharan Africa.

Debre Berhan Selassie church — the Church of the Trinity — has perhaps the most celebrated interior in Ethiopian Orthodox tradition: a ceiling painted entirely with the faces of angels, each one different.

## Highlights

- **Fasilides Castle** — the founding emperor's 17th-century royal residence
- **Royal Enclosure** — six castles in a UNESCO-listed walled compound
- **Debre Berhan Selassie church** — ceiling of 80 painted angel faces
- **Fasilides Bath** — flood for Timkat celebrations each January
- **Falasha village** — the village of Ethiopia's Jewish community

# Axum — Seat of Ancient Empire

**Region:** Northern Ethiopia · **Best time:** Year-round · **Recommended stay:** 1–2 nights

## Overview

Axum was the capital of one of the ancient world's great powers — a maritime trading empire that controlled the Red Sea trade routes from the 1st to 7th centuries AD. At its peak, the Axumite Empire was one of only four major powers in the world, alongside Rome, Persia, and China.

Today's Axum is a small town surrounded by extraordinary monuments: stelae fields of towering obelisks, underground royal tombs, the church that Ethiopians believe houses the original Ark of the Covenant, and a network of archaeological sites that have barely been excavated.

## Highlights

- **Great Stelae field** — towering carved obelisks, the tallest standing stele in the world at 24m
- **St Mary of Zion church** — contains the alleged Ark of the Covenant (not accessible)
- **Underground tombs** — the royal tombs of King Kaleb and King Gebre Meskel
- **Queen of Sheba's bath** — sacred pool used in Timkat ceremonies
- **Archaeological museum** — coins, inscriptions, and artefacts from the Axumite period

# Harar — The Walled City of 99 Mosques

**Region:** Eastern Ethiopia · **Best time:** Year-round · **Recommended stay:** 1–2 nights

## Overview

Harar is the fourth holiest city in Islam after Mecca, Medina, and Jerusalem. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site — a 16th-century walled city of 82 mosques, 102 shrines, and a labyrinth of narrow alleyways where Harari women in traditional dress carry baskets of qat to the market. Arthur Rimbaud, the French poet, lived here in the 1880s as a coffee trader. His house still stands.

The hyena feeding ritual is Harar's most famous attraction. Each night, a "hyena man" feeds wild spotted hyenas by hand at the old city walls. The tradition is said to be 500 years old.

## Highlights

- **Hyena feeding** — nightly ritual at the old city walls, wild hyenas fed by hand
- **Jugol walled city** — the old city, a labyrinth of 6km of walls and 5 gates
- **Rimbaud House** — the French poet's trading house, now a museum
- **Harari coffee ceremony** — the original coffee culture, elaborate three-round ceremony
- **Aw Bedir shrine** — one of the most important Islamic shrines in Africa

# Tigray — Churches Carved into Cliffs

**Region:** Northern Ethiopia · **Best time:** October–February · **Recommended stay:** 3–5 nights

## Overview

The Tigray region contains some of the most dramatic sacred architecture in the world — churches carved directly into cliff faces, accessible only by rope or narrow rock-cut ledges. Abuna Yemata Guh requires a 45-minute climb up a near-vertical rock face. The reward is a small, complete church painted with 15th-century frescoes, clinging to a cliff 900 metres above the valley floor.

These are not museum pieces. They are active churches where priests still celebrate Mass. The rock churches of Tigray predate Lalibela's and represent an older, more austere tradition of Christian architecture.

## Highlights

- **Abuna Yemata Guh** — the most dramatically situated church in the world, cliff-face access
- **Debre Damo monastery** — ancient monastery accessible only by leather rope, no women permitted
- **Gheralta rock churches** — cluster of 8 churches in a dramatic sandstone landscape
- **Yeha temple** — pre-Axumite temple from the 5th century BC, the oldest standing structure in Ethiopia

# Lake Tana — Source of the Blue Nile

**Region:** Amhara Region · **Best time:** Year-round · **Recommended stay:** 1–2 nights

## Overview

Lake Tana is Ethiopia's largest lake and the source of the Blue Nile. The lake is dotted with 37 islands, 20 of which contain Orthodox Christian monasteries. Some of these monasteries are over 600 years old and contain extraordinary collections of medieval illuminated manuscripts, gold processional crosses, and royal crowns.

The Blue Nile Falls — Tis Abay, "the smoke of fire" — are a short drive from Bahir Dar, the lakeside city. Reduced by hydroelectric diversion from their original 400-metre width, they remain one of Ethiopia's most visited natural sights.

## Highlights

- **Ura Kidane Mehret monastery** — the most ornate monastery on the lake, accessible by boat
- **Blue Nile Falls (Tis Abay)** — most powerful in Ethiopia's dry season
- **Papyrus tankwa boats** — traditional reed boats still used by fishermen
- **Hippos** — herds of hippos visible from the lake shore at dusk
- **Zeghie Peninsula monasteries** — cluster of ancient churches accessible only by boat

# Addis Ababa — Gateway to Ethiopia

**Region:** Central Ethiopia · **Best time:** Year-round · **Recommended stay:** 1–2 nights

## Overview

Addis Ababa — "New Flower" in Amharic — sits at 2,355 metres, making it the third highest capital city in the world. It is the headquarters of the African Union, the diplomatic capital of Africa, and a city of extraordinary contrasts: ancient markets and modern towers, traditional tej houses and specialty coffee bars, handwoven textiles and contemporary art galleries.

Most Ethiopia journeys begin and end in Addis. A day or two here is worth building in: the National Museum houses Lucy, the 3.2-million-year-old hominid skeleton; the Mercato is one of the largest open-air markets in Africa; and the coffee ceremony culture here reaches its most refined expression.

## Highlights

- **National Museum** — Lucy (Australopithecus afarensis) and extraordinary Ethiopian art collection
- **Mercato** — one of Africa's largest markets, spices, textiles, and everything else
- **Entoto hills** — eucalyptus forests above the city, views of the entire Addis basin
- **Specialty coffee** — Ethiopia is the birthplace of coffee; Addis has the best
- **Holy Trinity Cathedral** — Ethiopia's second most senior Orthodox church, burial site of Emperor Haile Selassie

# Awash National Park — Oryx and Hot Springs

**Region:** Afar Region · **Best time:** October–March · **Recommended stay:** 1–2 nights

## Overview

Awash National Park is Ethiopia's most accessible wildlife area — a 3-hour drive from Addis Ababa into the semi-arid Rift Valley. The park's combination of grassland, acacia woodland, and the Awash River gorge produces a surprisingly diverse wildlife experience. Beisa oryx, Soemmering's gazelle, and warthog are common; the river supports hippo and crocodile.

The Awash Falls — where the river plunges into a basalt gorge — are spectacular. The Filwoha hot springs at the park boundary are a luxury camp destination.

# Kaffa — Birthplace of Coffee

**Region:** Southern Ethiopia · **Best time:** October–January · **Recommended stay:** 2–3 nights

## Overview

The word "coffee" derives from "Kaffa" — the forest region in southwestern Ethiopia where Coffea arabica grows wild. According to legend, a 9th-century goat herder named Kaldi noticed his goats became unusually energetic after eating berries from a particular tree. The monks at a nearby monastery made a drink from the berries and found it kept them awake during evening prayers.

The Kaffa region today is UNESCO-recognised biosphere reserve. Walking through wild coffee forest — where the plants grow 5 metres tall under a closed canopy — is a genuinely extraordinary experience, particularly for anyone who drinks coffee daily without ever having seen the plant.

# Gambella — Nile Tributaries and Nilotic Cultures

**Region:** Western Ethiopia · **Best time:** November–March · **Recommended stay:** 3–4 nights

## Overview

Gambella is Ethiopia's most remote accessible region — a lowland, tropical zone bordering South Sudan where Nilotic peoples (the Anuak and Nuer) live in communities that have more in common culturally with South Sudan than highland Ethiopia. The region is remote, logistically challenging, and extraordinary.

The Gambella National Park contains one of Africa's most dramatic wildlife migrations: approximately 800,000 white-eared kob and tiang antelopes move through the park seasonally — a migration rivalling the Serengeti in scale but seen by almost no one.

# Harenna Forest — Cloud Forest of the South

**Region:** Southern Ethiopia (Bale Mountains) · **Best time:** Year-round (drier Oct–March) · **Recommended stay:** 1–2 nights (usually combined with Bale Mountains itinerary)

## Overview

The Harenna Forest occupies the southern slopes of the Bale Mountains, descending from 3,500m to 1,500m through a transition from Afroalpine moorland to montane cloud forest to lowland tropical forest. It is one of the largest remaining Afromontane forests in Africa and one of the most biodiverse.

The forest is home to African wild dogs, black-and-white colobus monkeys, lions (rarely seen), and an extraordinary range of endemic birds including the Ethiopian bush crow and Abyssinian ground thrush.

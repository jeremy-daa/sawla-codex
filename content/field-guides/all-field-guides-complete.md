# When to Visit Ethiopia — The Complete Month-by-Month Guide

Ethiopia has two seasons and no universally "best" month. The right time depends entirely on what you want to see and where you want to go.

## The Two Seasons

**Dry season (October–June):** The main travel window. Roads are accessible, skies are clear, wildlife is concentrated around water sources, and trekking conditions are at their best. The northern circuit — Lalibela, Axum, Gondar, Simien Mountains — is best October through April.

**Rainy season (June–September):** The highlands receive heavy rain, some roads become impassable, and the landscape turns vivid green. Far fewer tourists. Lower prices. Some experiences — particularly trekking and the Danakil — are impractical.

## Month by Month

**October:** The rains end. The landscape is green from the wet season but roads are drying. Excellent for the Simien Mountains, gelada viewing, and the northern circuit. Irreecha festival (Oromo thanksgiving) typically falls in October.

**November:** Peak season begins. Clear skies, comfortable temperatures, wildlife active. Best month for the Bale Mountains, Ethiopian wolves, and general touring. Book well in advance.

**December:** Excellent conditions everywhere. Christmas (Genna) falls on January 7th — preparing for it in December means festive atmosphere in Addis and the north. Danakil season begins.

**January:** Timkat (Ethiopian Epiphany) — the most spectacular festival in the Orthodox calendar. Falls on January 19th (January 20th in leap years). Lalibela during Timkat is one of the great spectacles in Africa. Book 9+ months in advance for this period.

**February:** Ideal conditions. Post-Timkat crowds have thinned. Danakil in best condition. Ethiopian wolf most active in Bale. Best birding months begin.

**March:** Still excellent. Wildflowers emerging on the Afroalpine plateaus. Temperatures warming slightly in the lowlands.

**April–May:** Ethiopian Easter (Fasika) — date varies, check annually. Second great festival season in Lalibela. End of the best period for Danakil (getting hot). Highlands still excellent.

**June:** Last good month before the rains. Some high-altitude areas still accessible. Temperatures mild.

**July–August:** Main rainy season. Some roads impassable. Trekking difficult. However: green landscapes, dramatic skies, almost no tourists, significantly lower prices. Good for Addis Ababa cultural visits and accessible destinations.

**September:** Rains ending. Meskel festival (Finding of the True Cross) — huge bonfire ceremony in Addis Ababa's Meskel Square, typically September 27th. Worth timing a trip around.

# Ethiopia Visa Requirements — Complete 2026 Guide

## eVisa (recommended for most nationalities)

Most international visitors can apply for an Ethiopian tourist visa online through the official eVisa portal. Processing typically takes 3–5 business days.

**Official portal:** https://www.evisa.gov.et (verify this is still current before applying)

**Required documents:**
- Valid passport (minimum 6 months validity beyond travel dates)
- Recent passport-sized photograph
- Return flight booking or onward travel evidence
- Accommodation confirmation for first night
- Visa fee (varies by nationality — check portal for current rates)

**Validity:** Single-entry tourist visa valid for 30 days, extendable inside Ethiopia.

## Visa on Arrival

Available at Bole International Airport in Addis Ababa for most nationalities, but the eVisa is strongly recommended to avoid queues on arrival.

## What Sawla Tours Provides

All Sawla Tours clients receive a detailed visa guidance document specific to their nationality as part of the booking process. We monitor visa policy changes and will advise if requirements change before your departure.

# Health & Safety in Ethiopia — Complete Guide

## Vaccinations

**Required:**
- Yellow fever: Required if arriving from or transiting through a yellow fever endemic country. Recommended for all visitors.

**Strongly recommended:**
- Hepatitis A and B
- Typhoid
- Tetanus, diphtheria, pertussis (ensure up to date)
- Rabies (recommended for anyone spending time outdoors)
- Meningococcal meningitis

**Consult your travel health clinic** at least 6–8 weeks before departure for personalised advice.

## Malaria

Malaria is present in Ethiopia below 2,000 metres. The following destinations require prophylaxis:
- Omo Valley
- Gambella
- Danakil Depression and Afar lowlands
- Awash National Park
- Any lowland areas

The following destinations are generally malaria-free (above 2,000m):
- Addis Ababa
- Lalibela
- Gondar
- Simien Mountains
- Bale Mountains

Consult your travel health provider for current prophylaxis recommendations.

## Altitude

Ethiopia's highland destinations sit at significant altitude:
- Addis Ababa: 2,355m
- Lalibela: 2,630m
- Simien Mountains: 3,000–4,550m
- Bale Mountains (Sanetti Plateau): 4,000m

**Altitude sickness** is a real risk above 3,000m, particularly on the Simien trek and Sanetti Plateau. Sawla Tours itineraries are designed with acclimatisation days. Descend immediately if you experience severe headache, confusion, or difficulty breathing.

## Food and Water

- Drink bottled or filtered water only
- Ice in restaurants may not be safe — specify no ice
- Cooked food from reputable restaurants is generally safe
- Raw vegetables and unpeeled fruit carry higher risk

## Security

The main tourist circuits — Lalibela, Gondar, Axum, Addis Ababa, Simien Mountains, Bale Mountains — are safe and visited by thousands of international tourists annually. Sawla Tours monitors security conditions continuously and will advise clients of any relevant changes. We only operate in areas with confirmed stable conditions.

# What to Pack for Ethiopia — The Complete List

Ethiopia spans four climate zones in a single itinerary — from 4,000m Afroalpine plateau to 130-metre-below-sea-level desert. Packing correctly means layering.

## Clothing

**For highlands (Lalibela, Simien, Bale, Gondar, Addis):**
- Warm fleece or down jacket — nights at altitude are cold year-round
- Thermal base layer for Simien/Bale trekking
- Light waterproof jacket (essential for Simien even in dry season)
- 2–3 light long-sleeve shirts (sun protection + mosquito protection in evenings)
- 1–2 pairs lightweight trekking trousers
- Comfortable walking shoes (trail runners adequate for most, hiking boots for Simien trek)

**For lowlands (Omo Valley, Danakil, Awash):**
- Light, loose, long-sleeved shirts and trousers (sun and insect protection)
- Wide-brim sun hat
- Sunglasses (UV protection essential — Danakil UV is extreme)
- Lightweight sandals for camp

**For churches and cultural sites:**
- Shoulders and knees must be covered at Orthodox churches — carry a light scarf
- Remove shoes before entering churches — flip-flops are practical

## Equipment

- Sunscreen SPF 50+ (hard to find outside Addis)
- Insect repellent (DEET-based for malaria areas)
- Personal water purification (Steripen or Sawyer) as backup
- Headlamp with spare batteries (essential for Erta Ale night hike)
- Power bank — charging opportunities limited in remote areas
- Camera with extra memory cards and batteries

## Medications

- All prescription medications in sufficient supply plus extra
- Malaria prophylaxis (if required — see health guide)
- Altitude sickness medication (Diamox) — consult doctor before travel
- Oral rehydration salts
- Imodium / antidiarrhoeal medication
- Broad-spectrum antibiotic (consult doctor for prescription)

# Ethiopia Photography Guide — Tips, Ethics & Practicalities

## The Opportunity

Ethiopia is one of the most photogenic countries in the world. Ancient churches with extraordinary interiors. Wildlife found nowhere else. Tribal cultures with elaborate visual traditions. Landscapes that shift from Afroalpine to volcanic desert to tropical forest within a single journey.

It also requires thoughtful, ethical practice.

## Cultural Photography Ethics

**The most important rule:** Ask before photographing people. In many parts of Ethiopia, particularly in the Omo Valley, people may request payment for photographs. This is not unusual — it is a legitimate economic arrangement. Our guides will help negotiate appropriate amounts.

**In the Omo Valley:** Photography of tribal people is a transaction. Accept this graciously. The rates are small and the experience is worth it. Never photograph ceremonies or intimate moments without explicit permission.

**At Orthodox churches:** Photography inside church sanctuaries is often prohibited or restricted to specific areas. Always ask the priest or guard before photographing. Priests in ceremonial vestments may be photographed but ask first. Respect is non-negotiable.

**General rule:** If in doubt, put the camera down and experience the moment. You will have other opportunities.

## Technical Notes

**For wildlife (Bale Mountains, Simien Mountains):**
- Ethiopian wolves at distance: 400–600mm effective focal length recommended
- Gelada baboons at close range: any lens works — 50–200mm ideal
- Dawn and dusk light in the Simien Mountains is extraordinary — fast glass (f/2.8 or wider) useful

**For churches (Lalibela, Gondar):**
- Interior light is low — ISO 1600–6400 often necessary
- Tripod or monopod useful but may require special permission
- Church interiors at dawn (4–6am) have beautiful natural light

**For landscapes (Danakil, Simien):**
- Golden hour is everything in Ethiopia — sunrise and sunset dramatically different from midday
- Danakil sulphur springs: colours most saturated in overcast light, midday sun bleaches them

# Ethiopian Culture & Etiquette — What Every Visitor Should Know

## Religion

Ethiopia is one of the oldest Christian nations in the world — the Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church was established in the 4th century. Islam is the second largest religion, strongest in the east and southeast. Religious life is central to Ethiopian culture.

**At Orthodox churches:** Dress modestly (shoulders and knees covered). Remove shoes before entering. Do not photograph without permission. If a service is in progress, observe quietly or return later.

**During fasting periods:** Ethiopian Orthodox Christians fast — meaning no animal products — on Wednesdays, Fridays, and during the main fasting seasons. Many restaurants offer fasting menus. This affects what food is available, particularly in smaller towns.

## Greetings

Ethiopians greet extensively. Taking time with greetings is important — rushing past them is considered rude. Handshakes are common. Close friends exchange cheek kisses. When in doubt, follow the lead of your guide.

## Food Culture

The coffee ceremony is a significant cultural institution. If offered, accept. The host roasts beans, grinds them, brews the coffee, and serves three rounds. Refusing after the first cup is acceptable; refusing the whole ceremony is not.

**Injera** — the fermented teff flatbread that forms the basis of Ethiopian cuisine — is eaten with the right hand. Left hand is considered unclean for food. Sharing a plate is normal and convivial.

## Tipping

Tipping is expected and important in Ethiopia. Our guides will advise on appropriate amounts. As a baseline: guides ETB 500–1,000/day; drivers ETB 300–500/day; hotel staff ETB 50–100 per service; restaurant staff 10% of bill.

# Ethiopia Birding Guide — 850+ Species, 18 Endemics

Ethiopia is one of the most important birding destinations in Africa. Its combination of highland, lowland, forest, and semi-arid habitats — many of them found only in Ethiopia — produces extraordinary species diversity, including 18 birds found nowhere else on earth.

## The 18 Ethiopian Endemics

1. **Ethiopian Wolf** — not a bird, but birders note the co-existence; see below
2. **Prince Ruspoli's Turaco** — Sidama region forests; one of the rarest birds in Africa
3. **Degodi Lark** — extreme southeast; only a handful of records
4. **Sidamo Lark** — Soda Plains south of Addis; critical, highly localised
5. **Harwood's Francolin** — Blue Nile valley
6. **Ankober Serin** — cliffs near Ankober; tiny range
7. **Ethiopian Siskin** — highland forests; uncommon
8. **Abyssinian Longclaw** — Bale Mountains plateau; reliable at Dinsho
9. **Spot-breasted Lapwing** — highland areas
10. **Yellow-fronted Parrot** — Harenna forest and southwest
11. **Black-winged Lovebird** — western highlands
12. **Rüppell's Chat** — rocky highlands; common
13. **White-tailed Swallow** — Yabello area; highly localised
14. **Bale Parisoma** — Bale Mountains; recently described species
15. **Ethiopian Bush Crow** — Yabello area; possibly the most bizarre endemic
16. **Stresemann's Bush Crow** — same as above (taxonomic synonym)
17. **Nechisar Nightjar** — known from a single wing; possibly extinct
18. **Salvadori's Serin** — Bale Mountains

## Best Birding Sites

**Bale Mountains:** The most productive birding site in Ethiopia. Sanetti Plateau for Abyssinian longclaw, Ethiopian wolf (not a bird but present), and Afroalpine specialists. Harenna forest for Yellow-fronted Parrot, Bale Parisoma.

**Yabello/Soda Plains:** Ethiopian Bush Crow (unlike any other bird in the world), White-tailed Swallow, Sidamo Lark.

**Lake Awasa/Lake Langano:** Waterbirds, pelicans, flamingo, fish eagle.

**Ankober:** Ankober Serin at the cliff edge.

# How to Plan an Ethiopia Trip — The Complete Guide

## Step 1: Define What You Want to Feel

Ethiopia is too diverse to approach as a single destination. The right question is not "what are the highlights?" but "what kind of experience do I want?" The answer determines everything else.

- Ancient history and sacred architecture → Northern circuit: Lalibela, Axum, Gondar, Tigray
- Wildlife and endemic species → Bale Mountains, Simien Mountains
- Cultural immersion, tribal encounter → Omo Valley
- Extreme geography, frontier adventure → Danakil Depression
- Festival timing → plan around Timkat (January) or Meskel (September)
- Photography, documentary experience → Cinematic Journey with Sawla Films

## Step 2: Decide on Duration

**7–10 days:** One region well. Northern circuit OR Simien + Lalibela. Cannot combine north and south adequately.

**10–14 days:** Two regions comfortably. Classic combination: Northern circuit + Omo Valley, or Northern circuit + Bale Mountains.

**14–21 days:** Three or more regions. The complete Ethiopia experience. Danakil + North + South, or North + Bale + Omo.

**21+ days:** Country-wide circuit. Everything including remote western regions, Kaffa coffee forest, Gambella migration.

## Step 3: Choose Your Season

See the When to Visit guide for full detail. Key constraint: **Danakil Depression November–February only.** Timkat (Lalibela) January only.

## Step 4: Consider Pacing

Ethiopia requires recovery time. The country is physically and emotionally demanding — not in a negative way, but in the way that profound experiences are demanding. Don't pack 10 destinations into 14 days. A well-paced 12-day itinerary covering three destinations will produce a deeper experience than a rushed 16-day circuit covering six.

# Ethiopia Accommodation Guide — From Guesthouses to Lodges

## The Range

Ethiopia's accommodation landscape has improved dramatically in the past decade. The main tourist centres now have excellent options across budget, mid-range, and comfortable categories. True luxury lodges remain scarce compared to East Africa, but this is changing.

## Addis Ababa

The capital has good international hotel options including Radisson Blu, Sheraton (the best in the country), Hilton, and a growing number of boutique guesthouses in the Bole and Kazanchis neighbourhoods. Business-class international hotels are competitively priced by global standards.

## Northern Circuit (Lalibela, Gondar, Axum)

**Lalibela** has the widest range: Ben Abeba (unique architecture, great views), Tukul Village (traditional style, good location), Mountain View Hotel, and Maribela are all reliable options. During Timkat, all properties fill completely — book a year in advance.

**Gondar** options include Goha Hotel (hilltop location, good views), Quara Hotel, and several smaller guesthouses in the town centre.

**Axum** has fewer options — Yeha Hotel and Remhai Hotel are the most reliable choices.

## Simien Mountains

The main trekking camps have simple lodges at Sankaber and Chenek. For those wanting more comfort, lodges near the park entrance at Debark are basic. Sawla Tours' mobile tented camp option provides a significant upgrade over standard accommodation on multi-day treks.

## Bale Mountains

Bale Mountain Lodge is the premier option — beautifully designed eco-lodge in the forest below the park. Dinsho Lodge at the park headquarters is simple but reliable. Sawla Tours operates mobile tented camps on the Sanetti Plateau for guests wanting to sleep at altitude.

## Omo Valley

Accommodation in the Omo Valley ranges from simple to basic. Jinka, Key Afer, and Turmi have acceptable guesthouses. Sawla Tours operates mobile tented camps in the Omo Valley for a significantly upgraded experience — camping among the acacia trees beside the river is one of the highlights of the trip.

# Ethiopian Food & Drink Guide

## Injera

Ethiopian cuisine centres on injera — a large, spongy flatbread made from fermented teff flour, with a mildly sour taste. It serves as both plate and utensil: dishes are placed on top of it, and diners tear off pieces to scoop up stews and salads.

Injera is eaten at almost every Ethiopian meal. If you enjoy fermented flavours (sourdough, kimchi), you will likely love it. If you find sour flavours difficult, it takes some adjustment. The flavour mellows with familiarity.

## The Main Dishes

**Tibs:** Sautéed meat (beef, lamb, or goat) with onions, peppers, and spices. The most common meat dish in Ethiopian restaurants.

**Doro wat:** Ethiopia's national dish — slow-cooked chicken in a rich berbere (spiced red pepper) sauce, served with a hard-boiled egg. Rich, complex, very satisfying.

**Kitfo:** Ethiopian steak tartare — raw or lightly cooked minced beef seasoned with mitmita (chilli) and niter kibbeh (spiced clarified butter). A delicacy.

**Fasting food (ye'som mager):** On fasting days (Wednesday, Friday, and throughout Ethiopian Orthodox Lent), no meat or dairy is served. Instead: misir (red lentil wat), gomen (collard greens), tikel gomen (cabbage), and various bean dishes. Extraordinarily good — some of the best food in Ethiopia.

## Coffee

Ethiopia is the birthplace of coffee. Teff-soil-grown Ethiopian arabica is among the finest in the world. The coffee ceremony — roasting beans over charcoal, grinding by hand, brewing in a clay pot, serving three rounds in small cups — is a cultural institution. Participate whenever possible.

## Drinks

**Tej:** Traditional honey wine (mead). Available in tej houses (tej bets) across Ethiopia. Sweetness varies enormously. Ask our guides where the best local tej house is.

**St George beer:** The most common Ethiopian beer, named after the patron saint. Light lager. Widely available.

**Water:** Drink bottled water only. Packaged water is available everywhere on the tourist circuit.

# Mobile, Internet & Connectivity in Ethiopia

## SIM Cards

Ethiopian Telecom (Ethio Telecom) and Safaricom Ethiopia are the two mobile operators. Both offer tourist SIM cards purchasable at Bole International Airport on arrival. Ethio Telecom has broader coverage across remote areas.

Data speeds are adequate for messaging and basic browsing in towns. Remote destinations (Simien Mountains, Bale Mountains, Danakil) have limited or no connectivity — plan accordingly.

## Internet Access

Wi-Fi is available at most hotels in Addis Ababa and the main tourist towns. Speed and reliability vary. Assume minimal connectivity in the Danakil and remote Omo Valley.

## Offline Preparation

Download the following before arriving in remote areas:
- Maps.me or Google Maps offline maps for Ethiopia
- Your itinerary and all accommodation confirmation emails
- Emergency contacts and insurance documents
- Sawla Tours WhatsApp number saved: +251 970 578 306

## Power

Ethiopia uses the Type C/E/F European round-pin plug (220V). Carry a universal adapter. Power cuts are common in smaller towns — carry a power bank.

## Currency

Ethiopian Birr (ETB) is the local currency. As of 2026, USD 1 ≈ ETB 120–130 (verify current rate). US dollars and euros are exchangeable at banks and the official airport bureau de change.

Credit cards are accepted at larger Addis Ababa hotels and some restaurants. Cash (ETB) is essential everywhere else. Carry sufficient ETB for the day's expenses before leaving major towns.

## ATMs

Available in Addis Ababa and most regional capitals (Bahir Dar, Gondar, Mekele, Hawassa). Not available in Lalibela (nearest in Dessie), Simien Mountains, or Danakil. Plan ahead.

## Tipping Budget

Budget ETB 500–1,000 per day for your guide, ETB 300–500 per day for your driver. Have clean ETB notes ready — tipping at the end of each day is standard.

# GUIDE 1: WHEN TO VISIT ETHIOPIA

**File:** /content/guides/when-to-visit-ethiopia.md

## The Short Answer

October to February is the best time for most Ethiopia trips — dry season, clear skies, accessible roads, festival season, and the best wildlife viewing conditions.

## Month-by-Month Guide

**October**  
The main rainy season ends and Ethiopia transforms. The landscape is at its most lush and green. Roads dry out progressively through the month. The Ethiopian New Year (Enkutatash) falls at the start of September — October is peak post-rain beauty. Good month for the Simien and Bale Mountains. Wildlife active; Ethiopian wolves visible on Sanetti Plateau.

**November**  
Excellent across all regions. Dry, clear, moderate temperatures. The best month for Danakil (before peak heat). Trekking in Simien and Bale is ideal. Omo Valley road conditions fully recovered from rains.

**December**  
Peak season begins. Perfect conditions across Ethiopia. Clear views from escarpments, best light for photography, roads fully open. Book domestic flights and Bale Mountain Lodge early.

**January**  
Timkat (Ethiopian Epiphany, usually 19–20 January) is the most spectacular festival in the Ethiopian calendar. Gondar and Lalibela host the largest celebrations. Plan around Timkat if dates allow.

**February**  
Still excellent. The Simien Mountains are at their clearest. Last dry month before any pre-rain haze begins. Good for photography — the highland light is at its best.

**March**  
Shoulder season begins. Some haze in the highlands. Still largely dry but some pre-rains are possible in the south. Good for travelers who want lower prices and fewer tourists in Lalibela.

**April**  
The belg (small rains) affect parts of central Ethiopia. Addis Ababa and the highlands can be wet. Still worthwhile but less predictable. Good for budget travelers.

**May**  
Hot and increasingly hazy. Pre-main-rains humidity in lowland areas. Danakil not recommended (extreme heat). Last viable month for Simien trekking before rains.

**June**  
Main rainy season begins. Most highland roads remain passable but slow. The Omo Valley becomes challenging. Lalibela churches are reached by 4WD with difficulty. Not recommended for most travelers.

**July / August**  
Heavy rains. Ethiopia is dramatically green and largely empty of tourists. Lalibela's churches, surrounded by pilgrims wearing white against green hills, is visually extraordinary — but the access is hard and weather unpredictable. Some specialist travelers come specifically for this reason.

**September**  
Meskel (Finding of the True Cross) on 27 September is celebrated with enormous bonfires and ceremonies nationwide. Addis Ababa hosts a large public celebration. The rains taper off; roads begin to open. The beginning of the best travel window.

## Festival Calendar

| Festival | Date | Where | Notes |
|----------|------|-------|-------|
| Enkutatash (New Year) | ~11 September | Nationwide | Flowers, new clothes, celebrations |
| Meskel | ~27 September | Addis Ababa, major towns | Huge bonfire ceremony |
| Timkat (Epiphany) | ~19 January | Gondar, Lalibela | Most spectacular festival |
| Fasika (Easter) | March/April (variable) | Lalibela especially | All-night liturgy |
| Kulubi Gabriel | 28 December, 28 July | Kulubi near Dire Dawa | Massive pilgrimage |

# GUIDE 2: HOW TO PLAN AN ETHIOPIA TRIP

**File:** /content/guides/how-to-plan-your-trip.md

## How Long Do You Need?

**5–7 days:** A focused trip. The northern historic circuit (Addis–Lalibela–Gondar–Bahir Dar) is the standard choice. Do it properly with 2 nights in Lalibela and 1 night each in Gondar and Bahir Dar.

**8–10 days:** More flexibility. Add the Simien Mountains (2 nights at Chenek) to the northern circuit, or substitute Bale Mountains for a wildlife focus.

**12–14 days:** The best all-round Ethiopia experience. Northern circuit + Simien OR Bale. Consider adding Harar (eastern Ethiopia) for a completely different atmosphere.

**18–21 days:** Comprehensive. Northern + southern combination. Northern historic circuit + Omo Valley + Bale Mountains + possible Danakil.

**21+ days:** Ethiopia completely. The above plus lesser-visited regions: Gambella, Kafa, Dorze highlands, Yabello.

## The Key Decisions

**North or south first?**  
If flying into Addis and out of Addis, start with the north (Lalibela, Gondar) and end with the south — or vice versa. Avoid backtracking through Addis unless your itinerary requires it.

**Fly or drive?**  
Ethiopia is large. Driving from Addis to Lalibela takes 10+ hours. A 1-hour domestic flight makes more sense for most travelers. The road between Addis and the Simien Mountains (via Gondar) is scenic and worthwhile if time permits.

**How many destinations?**  
The most common planning mistake is too many destinations, not enough time at each. Lalibela deserves 2 full days. The Simien Mountains deserve 2+ nights. The Omo Valley needs 4–5 days minimum. Plan for depth, not coverage.

## Domestic Flights

Ethiopian Airlines operates domestic routes from Addis Ababa (ADD) to:
- Lalibela (LLI): ~1 hour, 3–4 flights daily
- Gondar (GDQ): ~1 hour, 2–3 flights daily
- Mekelle (MQX): ~1 hour, 2–3 flights daily (gateway for Danakil/Tigray)
- Dire Dawa (DIR): ~1 hour (gateway for Harar)
- Robe (GOB): ~1.5 hours (gateway for Bale Mountains)

Book domestic flights through Sawla Tours at the planning stage — schedules change and advance booking is strongly recommended during peak season.

## Understanding Road Conditions

Ethiopian roads range from excellent (the main Addis–Gondar highway) to extremely rough (Danakil tracks, remote Omo Valley routes). Sawla Tours uses Land Cruisers for all journeys and routes accordingly. Road conditions are checked before every trip.

# GUIDE 3: SAFETY IN ETHIOPIA

**File:** /content/guides/safety-in-ethiopia.md

## Overview

Ethiopia is safe for the vast majority of travelers in the destinations where tourism is established. The key is understanding which regions require current advice and which are consistently accessible.

## Safe Regions (Currently Stable)

- **Addis Ababa** — Capital city, secure for tourists
- **Lalibela and Amhara Region (north)** — Consistently safe tourist area
- **Gondar and Bahir Dar** — Established, safe historic sites
- **Simien Mountains National Park** — Safe with park guide and armed scout (both required)
- **Bale Mountains National Park** — Safe, established
- **Harar** — Safe, established
- **Addis–Rift Valley lakes corridor** — Generally safe
- **Kafa and southwest Ethiopia** — Generally safe

## Regions Requiring Current Advice

- **Tigray region** — Check current Foreign Ministry advisories before travel; conditions have improved but monitor
- **Danakil Depression** — Requires local Afar escort and careful logistics management; Sawla Tours assesses conditions before confirming trips
- **Omo Valley (some border areas)** — Generally safe with local guides; avoid independent travel

## Practical Safety

**Altitude:** Many Ethiopia destinations are above 2,000m. Lalibela is at 2,630m, Simien Mountains above 3,600m, Sanetti Plateau above 4,000m. Allow acclimatisation time; descend if symptoms are severe.

**Food and water:** Drink bottled or treated water. Ethiopian food at established restaurants is generally safe. Street food carries more risk for travelers without prior exposure to local bacteria.

**Health:** Consult a travel health clinic 6–8 weeks before departure. Yellow fever vaccination may be required depending on countries visited before Ethiopia. Malaria prophylaxis is recommended for low-altitude areas.

**Vehicle safety:** Sawla Tours uses properly maintained Land Cruisers with experienced drivers. Ethiopian roads require correct vehicles. Do not accept local minibuses for long rural routes.

## Sawla Tours Approach

Our team checks current conditions for all regions before every trip. If a route or destination is unsafe on the dates you plan to travel, we will tell you before you book and offer alternatives. We never send travelers somewhere we would not go ourselves.

# GUIDE 4: ETHIOPIA VISA GUIDE

**File:** /content/guides/ethiopia-visa-guide.md

## eVisa (Recommended)

Ethiopia operates an official eVisa system at **www.evisa.gov.et**. Most nationalities can apply online before travel.

**Tourist visa:** 30 days, extendable once for an additional 30 days  
**Processing time:** Typically 3–5 business days; apply at least 2 weeks before travel  
**Cost:** Varies by nationality; check the official portal for current fees  
**Required:** Valid passport (6+ months remaining), recent photo, basic personal information, return or onward ticket

## On Arrival

Some nationalities are eligible for visas on arrival at Addis Ababa Bole International Airport. Check current eligibility with the Ethiopian Embassy or the official eVisa portal before traveling. On-arrival processing can be slow during peak hours.

## Visa Extension

Extensions can be processed at the Immigration Office in Addis Ababa. Sawla Tours can provide guidance on the extension process for travelers who wish to stay beyond 30 days.

## Important Notes

- **Yellow fever certificate** is required if entering from a yellow fever endemic country
- **Travel insurance** is strongly recommended and required by some lodges for Danakil and remote expeditions
- Check your own government's current travel advice for Ethiopia before finalizing plans

# GUIDE 5: WHAT TO PACK FOR ETHIOPIA

**File:** /content/guides/what-to-pack-for-ethiopia.md

## General Principles

Pack for multiple microclimates. Ethiopia combines equatorial heat (Danakil), highland cold (Simien above 3,000m), tropical humidity (Gambella), and temperate city conditions (Addis). The variation is significant.

## Clothing

**Layers are essential:**
- Lightweight base layer (merino wool works well across all conditions)
- Mid layer: fleece or soft-shell for highlands
- Outer layer: waterproof jacket (even in dry season, highlands can have afternoon showers)
- Sun protection: hat, lightweight long sleeves for UV at altitude

**For cultural sites (churches, mosques, ceremonies):**
- Head covering for women at Orthodox churches
- Shoulders covered (both men and women)
- Shoes that can be removed quickly (many sites are entered barefoot)
- Light trousers rather than shorts for church visits

**For the Danakil:**
- Light, breathable, full-coverage clothing (sun protection, not warmth)
- Sandals for camp; closed shoes for walking on volcanic rock
- No dark colours — they absorb heat

**For trekking (Simien, Bale):**
- Broken-in, waterproof trekking boots
- Warm jacket and thermal layers (temperatures below 0°C at night above 3,500m)
- Trekking poles (recommended)
- Gaiters (optional but useful in wet highland grass)

## Health and Hygiene

- Water purification (tablets or SteriPen) — for trekking routes
- Hand sanitiser — essential for remote travel
- Sunscreen (SPF 50+) — UV is intense at altitude
- Insect repellent — essential for lower altitude areas
- Any prescription medication in original packaging with documentation

## Photography Equipment

- Telephoto lens (200–400mm) for wildlife
- Polarising filter for high-altitude sky photography
- Extra batteries and cards (charging points unreliable in remote areas)
- Note: Professional filming equipment, certain drones, and satellite devices may require advance permits or customs documentation. Notify Sawla Tours before arrival.

# GUIDE 7: ETHIOPIAN FOOD & COFFEE

**File:** /content/guides/ethiopian-food-coffee.md

## Injera: The Foundation

Ethiopian food is built around injera — a large, spongy sourdough flatbread made from teff (an ancient grain endemic to Ethiopia). The injera serves as plate, utensil, and bread simultaneously. Food is placed on it and eaten by tearing off pieces and using them to scoop stews, salads, and curds.

First-time injera eaters are divided. The sourness is pronounced; the texture is unlike anything familiar in European or American food. By day three, most travelers are eating it with genuine appetite.

## Key Dishes

**Tibs** — Sautéed meat (usually lamb or beef) with vegetables. The most reliably excellent dish for most travelers.

**Kitfo** — Raw or lightly cooked minced beef seasoned with mitmita (a chilli blend) and niter kibbeh (spiced clarified butter). A Gurage speciality. Exceptional when prepared well.

**Shiro** — Slow-cooked chickpea or broad bean flour stew. The standard vegetarian option; ubiquitous and often very good.

**Wat** — General term for stew. Doro wat (chicken stew with whole hard-boiled egg) is the national dish and particularly good for celebrations.

**Beyaynetu** — The fasting platter. Ethiopian Orthodox Christians fast (eat vegan) on over 200 days per year; beyaynetu is a collection of vegan dishes on one injera. Usually excellent — some of the best Ethiopian food is fasting food.

## The Coffee Ceremony

Ethiopia is the origin of coffee. The word coffee itself probably derives from Kaffa — a region in southwest Ethiopia where Coffea arabica grows wild.

The coffee ceremony (bunna) is conducted by the woman of the household and takes approximately one hour. Green beans are roasted over charcoal, ground by hand in a wooden mortar, brewed in a clay pot (jebena), and served in small cups — typically three rounds, each progressively weaker, with incense burning throughout.

Being invited to a coffee ceremony is the most genuine hospitality gesture in Ethiopian culture. Your guide will coach you on the appropriate response (you should accept, stay for all three rounds, and compliment the host).

## Tej and Other Drinks

**Tej** — Honey wine, brewed with gesho (buckthorn), served in distinctive flask-shaped glasses. Tastes like strong mead with a bitter finish. Traditional tej houses exist in most Ethiopian cities.

**Tella** — Home-brewed sorghum beer, very low alcohol, slightly sour. Drunk from clay pots in rural areas.

**Filtered coffee (Addis Ababa)** — The coffee culture in Addis has produced a generation of excellent independent coffee shops serving extraordinary Ethiopian single-origin coffee. Several roasters in Bole, Kazanchis, and Sarbet are worth finding.

# GUIDE 8: ETHIOPIA PHOTOGRAPHY GUIDE

**File:** /content/guides/ethiopia-photography-guide.md

## Light

Ethiopia's highland light is exceptional. At altitudes above 2,000 metres, the air is thin and clear; the golden hour stretches longer and the quality of light is sharper and warmer than at lower elevations.

**Best light windows:** 6:00–8:00am and 4:00–6:00pm  
**Worst light:** 10:00am–2:00pm (harsh, flat, bleaches colour)

Plan active photography around the light. This means early starts (worth it in the Simien Mountains) and staying in locations through the afternoon rather than driving.

## People Photography

This is the subject that requires the most careful thought.

**The principle:** Nobody owes you their photograph. Photography of people — especially in culturally distinctive communities like the Omo Valley, during festivals, or at churches — should be preceded by a human interaction, not just a camera pointed in someone's direction.

**In practice:**
- Always ask (your guide facilitates this) before photographing individuals
- Understand that some people will ask for payment — this is their right
- If someone declines, decline gracefully
- In the Omo Valley: work only through guides who have established community relationships; photography without proper context degrades the encounter for everyone who comes after you

**What makes the best photographs:** Patience. The strongest Ethiopia portraits come from a genuine exchange, not a transaction. Give your guide time to establish a connection before cameras appear.

## Technical Notes

**Altitude:** At 3,600m+ (Simien, Sanetti Plateau), UV is extreme. Use a UV filter; skin sunscreen is not optional.

**Dust:** Ethiopian roads are dusty in the dry season. Keep camera bags closed between shoots. Bring lens cloths and a blower.

**Wildlife photography:** For Ethiopian wolves, geladas, and birds, a telephoto lens (200–400mm equivalent or longer) dramatically improves results. The animals may come close, but behavioural shots — a wolf hunting, a gelada male displaying — happen at distance.

**Church interiors:** Flash is prohibited in most Ethiopian churches. A fast lens (f/1.8 or f/2.8) and high ISO capability are essential. The candlelit interiors of Lalibela's churches are extraordinary subjects if you have the right equipment.

## Professional Equipment

Drones, professional filming rigs, satellite communication devices, and some high-powered optical equipment may require advance permissions or customs documentation in Ethiopia. Notify Sawla Tours before arrival with a complete equipment list — we can advise on what requires pre-clearance.

# GUIDE 9 — GENERAL TRAVEL TIPS

**File:** /content/guides/general-travel-tips.md

## Currency and Money

The Ethiopian Birr (ETB) is the official currency. US Dollars are widely accepted for tour payments and in larger hotels. ATMs are available in Addis Ababa and major towns — always carry sufficient cash for rural travel where ATMs are absent. Credit cards are rarely accepted outside Addis.

## Communications

Mobile networks (Ethio Telecom, Safaricom Ethiopia) cover major routes and towns. Remote areas — Danakil, deep Omo Valley, highland trekking — have no coverage. Sawla Tours staff carry satellite communication devices on expeditions to remote areas.

SIM cards are available at the airport and in Addis; purchase with a passport.

## Electricity

220V, 50Hz. EU round-pin plugs (Type C/E/F) are standard. Voltage fluctuates in some areas; protect sensitive electronics with surge protectors.

## Cultural Etiquette

- Remove shoes before entering churches and mosques
- Cover head and shoulders at religious sites (women: headscarf; men: no shorts)
- Accept coffee when offered — refusing is mildly rude
- Eat with your right hand
- Ask before photographing people (see photography guide)
- Bargaining is normal in markets but should not be aggressive
- Tipping: customary for guides (10–15% of tour cost), drivers (equivalent), and service staff

## Altitude

Many Ethiopia destinations are above 2,000m. Lalibela sits at 2,630m; Gondar at 2,133m; Addis Ababa at 2,355m. The Simien escarpment and Sanetti Plateau go above 4,000m.

Symptoms of altitude sickness: headache, nausea, dizziness, disturbed sleep. Prevention: ascend slowly, drink water, avoid alcohol in first 24 hours, don't push through severe symptoms.

Sawla Tours itineraries are designed with altitude acclimatisation built in. If you have a history of altitude sickness, notify us during planning.

## Photography and Respect

See the dedicated photography guide. Summary: ask before you photograph people; understand that cultural communities deserve the same dignity you would give subjects at home.

## Language

Amharic is the official language; English is understood in cities and tourist areas. In rural areas, only Amharic or local languages may be spoken — your guide bridges this gap. Learning a few Amharic phrases (Selam — hello; Ameseginallehu — thank you; Betam — very/much) is appreciated and often produces genuine warmth.

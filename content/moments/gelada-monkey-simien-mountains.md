Nobody tells you about the sound first.

Before you see the geladas, you hear them — a low, rolling chorus of lip-smacks, grunts, and what field researchers call "gelada wobbles," a contact call that sounds like several dozen people doing a quiet impression of a boiling kettle. It travels up from the cliff edge before you reach it, and it's unlike anything you'll hear anywhere else on earth.

Then you crest the ridge, and there they are.

Not three or four. Not a photogenic dozen arranged on a rock. Six hundred geladas, maybe more, spread across a hillside of highland grass, working methodically through their morning feed. Infants cling to mothers' stomachs, their tiny red chest patches like pressed flowers. Males display to one another — bouncing, lip-curling, revealing a flash of pink gum — while a cluster of juveniles chase each other around a basalt outcrop with the particular energy of small animals who have not yet learned caution.

You are not behind a fence. You are not in a vehicle. You are standing three metres away from a one-unit-leader male, and he is completely uninterested in you.

### The world's only grass-eating primate

Geladas (*Theropithecus gelada*) are sometimes called "bleeding heart monkeys" for the bright red patch of bare skin on their chests, which flushes more deeply during social displays. They are not closely related to baboons, despite the superficial resemblance. They are their own ancient lineage — the last surviving species of a genus that once ranged across Africa — and they exist today only in Ethiopia's highland grasslands above 1,800 metres.

What makes them genuinely strange is what they eat. While most primates are opportunistic feeders, geladas are specialists: around 90% of their diet is grass. Specifically the blade, the stem, and the seed head. They work through a hillside with both hands in a fluid, bilateral plucking motion that researchers estimate they repeat up to 8,000 times a day. Watching a large troop feed is oddly meditative — hundreds of animals moving slowly across a slope, the grass parting around them, the mist coming and going on the escarpment below.

### Where you actually see them

The Simien Mountains National Park is the most reliable place. Specifically, the area around **Chenek** camp at around 3,600 metres is where most encounters happen, often immediately after you leave your tent in the morning. The geladas sleep on the cliff faces — safe from most predators — and climb up onto the plateau at dawn to feed.

**Sankaber**, lower in the park, also has good gelada viewing and is accessible on day trips from Debark if you're not staying overnight.

A few things worth knowing before you go:

The geladas here are habituated to human presence, meaning they have encountered enough rangers, researchers, and travelers over the years that they no longer treat people as a threat. This is not the same as tame. They are wild animals with their own social structures, their own hierarchies, and their own moments of volatility. Your guide will tell you not to make direct eye contact with the dominant males — sustained eye contact is a challenge display in gelada social language — and it is worth listening.

The encounter happens on foot, at the geladas' level. This is different from vehicle-based wildlife viewing. The animals move around you. A young male might walk within arm's reach to get to a better patch of grass. An infant might briefly investigate your boot before its mother pulls it away. There is no barrier, no window, no mediation. This is one of the few places on earth where you can sit quietly in the middle of a large primate troop and simply watch how they live.

### What the light does in the Simien Mountains

Any guide who has worked in the Simien for long enough will tell you: the quality of light here is unlike anywhere else in Ethiopia. The high altitude — most of the main tourist circuit sits between 3,200 and 4,000 metres — means the air is thin and clear, and the quality of light in the early morning and late afternoon has a particular warmth and sharpness that photographers travel specifically to find.

The red-chested males photograph extraordinarily well in that light. The contrast between the rust-orange chest patch, the dark face, and the silver-tipped mane creates a portrait that is both prehistoric and strangely regal.

If you have a camera, bring a lens with reach. The geladas may come close, but the best behavioural moments — a male display, a mother-infant interaction, a troop moving across a ridge — often happen at distance, and a 200–400mm equivalent will serve you better than a wide angle. If you are bringing specialist photographic equipment, speak to Sawla Tours before your trip. Some equipment — certain drones, professional filming rigs, powerful spotting scopes — may require advance permissions or customs documentation in Ethiopia.

### Getting the timing right

Geladas are most active in the morning, after they climb from the cliff faces to the feeding grounds. The window from shortly after dawn to around 10am is typically the best for both quantity of activity and quality of light. By midday they have often moved to shadier areas and become less active.

The best months for Simien trekking and wildlife viewing are broadly **October through February** — after the main rains have cleared, when the highland vegetation is at its greenest and the air is at its clearest. March through May can be hazy and humid before the rains. June through September, the main rainy season, makes trekking harder and reduces visibility, though the landscape becomes dramatically lush and some travelers specifically prefer the emptier trails.

For gelada viewing specifically, year-round access is possible — the geladas do not migrate — but road conditions and trek access are more reliable in the dry season.

### The rest of the Simien

Geladas are the most reliable wildlife encounter in the Simien Mountains, but they are not the only one. The park is also home to the **Walia ibex**, a large endemic wild goat found nowhere else, most often seen on steep rocky terrain in the northern sections of the park near Chenek. Ethiopian wolves pass through higher altitudes, though sightings are not guaranteed from the main trekking circuit. The escarpment itself hosts lammergeier vultures — the so-called bearded vulture, with a wingspan of over two metres — riding the thermals off the cliff face in the afternoons.

The views are, in a word, staggering. The Simien escarpment drops nearly 1,500 metres in places, and on a clear morning the escarpment edge at Chenek looks east across a void that seems too large to be real. This is why hikers come. The geladas are why they stay, and why they come back.

### Planning a Simien Mountains trip with Sawla Tours

The Simien Mountains work well as a standalone destination, or as part of a northern Ethiopia circuit combining Gondar, Lalibela, Bahir Dar, and Axum. A minimum of two nights in the park — ideally at Chenek — gives you the space for meaningful wildlife encounters without rushing. Three to four nights allows time for the longer trekking routes and the higher ground.

Most travelers fly to Gondar from Addis Ababa on Ethiopian Airlines, then drive two to three hours to Debark and into the park. The drive from Debark to Chenek takes another one to two hours depending on conditions.

All trekking in the Simien Mountains National Park is conducted with a licensed guide and an armed scout — a park regulation rather than an optional add-on.

Most itineraries start with a gap.

Not a gap in the traveler's knowledge — they've usually done their research. The gap is between what they think they want and what, after a conversation, they actually need from the trip.

This is a story about one itinerary. We've changed the identifying details, but the decisions are real. We tell it because the planning process is often invisible — travelers see the finished route and assume it arrived whole. It rarely does.

---

### The brief

The travelers were a couple from Germany. Both in their mid-fifties. One was a historian; the other had spent twenty years working in conservation in southern Africa and had a serious interest in endemic species. They had ten days from Addis Ababa, flying in and out on the same airport. They wanted history, at least one good wildlife encounter, and — this was the phrase that mattered — "enough room to actually experience what we're seeing, not just photograph it."

They were not interested in rushing. They had been to Kenya twice, to Rwanda, to Uganda. Ethiopia was different. They knew it intellectually, but they weren't sure how.

The initial email was three paragraphs long. That's usually enough to start.

---

### The first draft

Here is approximately what their first proposed itinerary looked like, before any refinement:

**Day 1:** Arrive Addis Ababa, city tour  
**Days 2–3:** Fly Gondar, Gondar Fasil Ghebbi and Debre Berhan Selassie church  
**Day 4:** Drive Simien Mountains, Chenek camp  
**Day 5:** Simien trekking and gelada encounter  
**Day 6:** Fly Lalibela  
**Days 7–8:** Lalibela rock-hewn churches, Asheton Maryam monastery  
**Day 9:** Fly Addis, afternoon rest  
**Day 10:** Fly home  

This is a competent northern circuit. It covers the main historic sites. It includes wildlife. It fits in ten days.

It was wrong for this couple.

---

### The problem with the first draft

The historian wanted depth at the sites, not coverage. Two days in Lalibela at that pace — with the monastery visit competing with church time and a half-day lost to the inbound flight — would have felt rushed. Lalibela done properly, with a knowledgeable guide who understands the liturgical calendar and can explain the specific theological meaning of each church's orientation, takes at least two full days and benefits from a third.

The conservationist had asked for a wildlife encounter. One day in the Simien — even a very good one — cannot reliably deliver the Ethiopian wolf, which was the endemic mammal she specifically wanted to see. Ethiopian wolves live in the Bale Mountains, not the Simien, where the main wildlife encounter is geladas and Walia ibex. The draft was built around the assumption that "wildlife" meant one place, when in fact the endemic mammal she wanted was in a completely different park.

Two things needed to change: the Simien wildlife day needed to become a gelada and Walia ibex day (which it could do well), and the route needed to make room for either Bale Mountains or a genuine decision about whether to go.

---

### The conversation

We went back with questions rather than a revised itinerary. This is important. The revision can't come before the understanding.

Three questions:
1. Between the castles of Gondar and more depth in Lalibela, which matters more to you?
2. Is the Ethiopian wolf a priority or a nice-to-have? Adding Bale means adding a domestic flight and at least two nights, which changes the whole structure.
3. You said "enough room to breathe" — what does that mean in practice? Are you comfortable with a packed first day, or do you need arrivals to be gentle?

The answers came back the same day:
1. Lalibela, without question. Gondar is interesting but we can read about it.
2. The wolf is a priority. Can you make it work?
3. We would rather have a rest day built in than a transfer day that pretends to be a rest day.

---

### The second draft

The second draft looked completely different:

**Day 1:** Arrive Addis Ababa. Airport transfer, rest afternoon. No city tour.  
**Day 2:** Addis — half-day National Museum and Merkato, late afternoon Ethiopian coffee ceremony.  
**Day 3:** Fly Gondar, afternoon Fasil Ghebbi. One site, done well.  
**Day 4:** Drive Simien Mountains. Afternoon arrival at Chenek, short acclimatisation walk.  
**Day 5:** Full day Simien — geladas, Walia ibex on rocky terrain, escarpment views. Return Debark, fly Lalibela.  
**Day 6:** Lalibela — northern group of churches with guide, at pace.  
**Day 7:** Lalibela — eastern and western groups, Asheton Maryam monastery, afternoon free.  
**Day 8:** Fly Addis, connect to Bale Mountains via Robe.  
**Day 9:** Bale Mountains — Sanetti Plateau, Ethiopian wolf with specialist guide.  
**Day 10:** Morning in Bale, fly Addis, international connection.  

This itinerary added a domestic flight. It took Gondar from two days to one. It put a rest day at the beginning instead of the end. And it built in the wolf as a genuine priority rather than an afterthought.

The conservationist read it and wrote back: "This is the trip."

---

### What nearly went wrong

The Bale section had a problem we almost missed.

Day 10 required a morning in Bale, a morning domestic flight from Robe to Addis, and an international connection from Addis. Ethiopian Airlines has one flight from Robe to Addis that arrives in the early afternoon. International connections from Addis require check-in at least three hours before departure. That left a margin of roughly ninety minutes — workable if everything ran perfectly, a disaster if it didn't.

We flagged this with the couple and offered two options: fly home a day later and build in a genuine buffer night in Addis, or accept the tight connection with the understanding that a missed international flight was a real possibility if the domestic flight was delayed (which happens).

They chose to add a day. The final itinerary had eleven days, not ten, with a hotel night in Addis before the international flight. It cost them one additional day of leave. It removed a significant logistical risk from the end of what was otherwise a well-built trip.

This kind of trade-off — the kind that travel platforms and fixed packages don't flag because they're not paying attention — is the part of the planning process that matters most. Anyone can put names on a page. Knowing which connections are tight, which roads are slow in October, which sites need to be visited early in the morning before the tour groups arrive — that's the work.

---

### What they said afterwards

The wolf sighting happened on day nine, mid-morning on the Sanetti Plateau. Two animals, working through a patch of grass about forty metres away. The conservationist watched them for twenty minutes through binoculars without speaking. When she turned to her guide, she said: "I've waited thirty years for that."

The historian spent an unplanned extra hour in the Bete Medhane Alem church in Lalibela on the second morning because the guide started explaining the symbolism of the twelve columns and neither of them wanted to stop. It was, she said later, one of the best hours of any trip she had taken anywhere.

The Addis buffer night was calm. The international flight was on time. The connection was straightforward.

### What this means for your trip

No two Ethiopia itineraries should look identical, because no two travelers are the same. The version that worked for this couple — starting slowly, prioritising depth over coverage, adding a domestic flight to reach a specific wildlife moment — would be completely wrong for a family traveling with teenagers, or a first-time Africa visitor who needs a broader introduction, or a photographer who needs four days in the Omo Valley.

The planning conversation is not a formality. It's the trip.

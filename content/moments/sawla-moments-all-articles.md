# Watching 800 Gelada Monkeys Wake Up on the Simien Escarpment

**Category:** Wildlife  
**Read time:** 8 min  
**Location:** Simien Mountains, Ethiopia  
**Author:** Sawla Tours field team

---

Before sunrise, the Simien Mountains are completely silent. The escarpment at Chenek sits at 3,600 metres, and at 5am in November the air is cold enough to see your breath. You walk from the camp to the cliff edge in darkness, guided by a torch, and you hear them before you see them — a low, rolling sound like distant conversation, rising from the rock faces below.

The gelada monkeys sleep on cliff ledges. They descend each evening to roost on faces that no predator can reach, and at dawn they emerge. On this particular morning, the herd is approximately 800 individuals — a number that seems impossible until you see it.

They come up the cliff in waves. Mothers carrying infants. Males with flowing capes of chest hair, the red hourglass patch on their chest catching the first light. Young animals tumbling and chasing each other across the moorland. Within thirty minutes of sunrise, the entire plateau above Chenek is alive with movement.

## The Sound of a Gelada Herd

Geladas are the most vocal of all primates relative to their social complexity. Each animal produces a continuous stream of calls — a "wobble" sound made by rapidly modulating the lips — that researchers have compared structurally to human speech more closely than any other animal vocalization. A herd of 800 animals produces a sound that is genuinely difficult to describe: not a roar, not a chatter, but something between a crowd of people murmuring and a river moving over stones.

You stand in this sound and it surrounds you completely.

## The Morning Routine

The herd moves slowly. Geladas are grass-eaters — they harvest individual grass blades with shortened, precision-grip fingers — and the morning is spent feeding. Males sit with their harems, occasionally displaying to challenge rivals. Juveniles wrestle. Infants ride on their mothers' backs, occasionally dismounting to investigate a particularly interesting tuft of grass before scrambling back.

At around 9am, the herd begins to consolidate. Large mixed groups come together into what researchers call "sleeping bands" — aggregations of up to several thousand animals that rest together at midday before the afternoon feeding session. To watch this aggregation is to see something that looks more like a migration than a morning feeding session: columns of animals moving across the plateau, converging on a central area, the sound rising as more animals arrive.

## What You Actually See

The thing that makes the Simien Mountains different from almost any other wildlife experience in Africa is the combination of drama and proximity. The escarpment landscape is genuinely breathtaking — cliff faces dropping 1,500 metres to the valley floor, the lowlands visible in haze far below. And the geladas are here, in this landscape, in large numbers, close enough to see the expression on their faces.

Geladas have expressive faces. The skin around their eyes and mouth is mobile and communicative. When a male confronts a rival, he lifts his upper lip to reveal his gums — a threat display that looks uncomfortably human. When a female grooms a companion, she works with focused attention. When a juvenile falls asleep in a patch of sunlight, curled against its mother, you feel something it is difficult to name.

We have been running trips to the Simien Mountains for seventeen years. The gelada herd at Chenek never stops being extraordinary.

# Why Ethiopia Is Not a Safari Destination — And Why That's the Point

**Category:** Perspective  
**Read time:** 6 min  
**Author:** Bemnet Alemu, Founder, Sawla Tours

---

Every year, someone arrives in Ethiopia having read that it is "Africa's best-kept secret." They are expecting something like Kenya or Tanzania — a landscape of savanna, a big-five checklist, a tented camp with a wine list. They find something completely different.

Ethiopia is not a safari destination. It is something stranger and, I think, more interesting.

## What People Expect

The mental model most international travellers bring to Africa is the Serengeti model: wildlife in open grassland, viewed from a vehicle, at some remove from human activity. It is a model refined by decades of nature television and a hundred years of safari industry infrastructure. It works beautifully in East Africa. It has almost nothing to do with Ethiopia.

Ethiopia's wildlife is mostly endemic — species found nowhere else — and mostly visible in landscapes that are also inhabited: Afroalpine moorland farmed by agropastoralists, highland forests where coffee grows wild, savanna grasslands crossed by cattle. The Ethiopian wolf hunts giant mole rats on a plateau grazed by sheep. The gelada baboons sleep on cliffs beside villages. The mountain nyala feeds in heathland where farmers have grown barley for four thousand years.

This is not a failure of separation. It is the reality of a country that has been continuously inhabited and farmed for longer than almost anywhere on earth.

## What Ethiopia Actually Is

Ethiopia is the only African country that was never colonised. It has 3,000 years of continuous recorded civilisation. It has its own alphabet, its own calendar (13 months), its own time system (the day starts at dawn, not midnight). It has 80+ languages and 16 distinct cultural regions. It is the birthplace of coffee, the birthplace of the Orthodox Christian church in Africa, and the setting of legends that have shaped world history.

When people come here expecting a safari and instead encounter all of this, there are two possible reactions. Some are disappointed — they wanted zebra and they found ancient rock churches. Others have their understanding of what travel can be completely recalibrated.

We try to make sure our clients are in the second group before they arrive.

## The Question We Ask

At Sawla Tours, the first question we ask every potential client is not "what do you want to see?" It is "what do you want to feel?"

Humbled by history? We take you to Lalibela at 4am during Timkat, when 10,000 white-robed pilgrims fill the excavated courtyards by candlelight. Astonished by wildlife? We take you to the Bale Mountains at dawn to watch Ethiopian wolves hunt on a plateau that looks like another planet. Confronted by the complexity of human culture? We take you to the Omo Valley, where sixteen ethnic groups live in the same river valley, each with entirely different languages, rituals, and aesthetics.

The experience of Ethiopia is not what you see. It is the process by which it dismantles your assumptions about what Africa is, what civilisation is, what ancient means, what wild means.

## Why We Do This

I started Sawla Tours in 2009, after years of watching international visitors come to Ethiopia and leave with a fraction of what was available to them — not because they weren't trying, but because nobody had organised the experience in a way that matched the country's actual character.

Ethiopia is not a backdrop. It is the foreground. The country is the experience.

That is why our guides are historians as much as naturalists. Why our itineraries mix church visits with wildlife mornings. Why we take clients to remote plateaus and to city markets and to coffee ceremonies in the same week. And why, every year, we have clients who return — not to see something they missed, but because Ethiopia is the kind of place you need to visit more than once to begin to understand.

# How We Planned a 17-Day Circuit Around a Single Conversation

**Category:** Behind the journey  
**Read time:** 5 min  
**Author:** Sawla Tours planning team

---

She called in October. She said she had been to Kenya three times, Tanzania twice, Botswana once. She said she wanted something that would make her feel the way she felt on her first safari — before she knew what to expect.

"I want to feel small," she said. "In a place that has survived everything."

This is the journey we built.

## The Conversation

The first thirty minutes of any planning conversation at Sawla Tours is not about itineraries. It is about the person. What they have already seen. What they are afraid of. What they are willing to be uncomfortable for. What would make them say, at the end, that this was the most important journey they have ever made.

She had never been to Ethiopia. She knew almost nothing about it. She had a vague sense of "ancient" and "authentic" — words that tell us almost nothing, because every destination uses them. What she meant, as we talked, was this: she wanted to be somewhere that had not been organised for her arrival. Somewhere that was itself regardless of whether she was there.

## The Constraint

She had seventeen days. She wanted to avoid group travel entirely. She was physically fit — had trekked to Everest Base Camp — but didn't want the journey to be primarily physical. And she said, almost as an afterthought, that she had been reading about Ethiopian wolves and wanted to see one if at all possible.

That last detail changed everything.

## The Architecture

Ethiopian wolves require the Bale Mountains — specifically the Web Valley and the Sanetti Plateau in the south of the country. The classic Northern Historical Circuit (Lalibela, Axum, Gondar, Tigray) is in the north. Connecting both in seventeen days requires either two domestic flights or an overland route through the Rift Valley, which takes two days but passes through some of the most beautiful lake scenery in Africa.

We chose the overland route. We gave her five days in the north, two days in the Rift Valley lakes, four days in the Bale Mountains, and the remaining time for Addis Ababa and travel.

## Day 1–5: The North

We started in Axum. Not Lalibela — which is where most circuits begin — because Axum is older, more austere, and because she had said she wanted to feel the weight of time. Axum is the correct place for that. The stelae field at dawn, when the light hits the carved obelisks at a low angle, produces a silence that is not tourist silence but the silence of a place that existed for two thousand years before you arrived and will continue after you leave.

From Axum she flew to Lalibela, spent two nights, and visited the churches both by day and at the morning liturgy — beginning at 4am, with priests in embroidered vestments chanting in Ge'ez, the ancient liturgical language, while pilgrims filled the excavated courtyards. She described it later as the most overwhelming three hours of her life.

## Day 6–8: The Rift Valley

The drive south from Addis Ababa through the Rift Valley lakes is a journey through a different Ethiopia — lower, drier, with flamingos on alkaline lakes and fish eagles on freshwater ones. We stopped at Langano for a night — the only swimmable lake in the Rift Valley — and at Hawassa for the morning fish market, which is one of the most chaotic and photogenic scenes in the country.

## Day 9–13: Bale Mountains

The Ethiopian wolves were seen on day two. At 6:30am in the Web Valley, in low golden light, three wolves were visible simultaneously — a female hunting alone, and a pair moving together across the valley floor. Our guide Tadesse pointed to a fourth, stationary on a ridge above, watching. Four wolves in the same field of view, in a landscape so large and silent that you had to remind yourself you were still on earth.

She watched for two hours without speaking.

On the Sanetti Plateau, at 4,000 metres, we walked across an Afroalpine moorland that felt more like Scotland than Africa — brown heather, rocky outcrops, a cold wind coming from the southeast. The mountain nyala were there, and the giant lobelia plants reaching 5 metres, and the occasional pair of thick-billed ravens tumbling in the wind above the plateau edge.

## The Last Conversation

On the final evening in Addis Ababa, she said: "I didn't expect it to feel like this. I kept waiting for it to be like the other places. It never was."

That is the sentence we work toward. Not "it was beautiful" — beautiful is everywhere. Not "it was authentic" — that word means nothing. But the specific recognition that what she had experienced was not like the other places, and that the difference was not a matter of degree but of kind.

Ethiopia does this reliably. If you arrive with the right question, it answers it.

# Ethiopian Wolf
## *Canis simensis* · The World's Rarest Canid

**IUCN Status:** Endangered  
**Population:** ~500 individuals  
**Endemic:** Yes — found only in Ethiopia  
**Best place to see:** Bale Mountains (Web Valley), Simien Mountains  
**Best time:** October–March (active during daylight hours year-round)

### Overview

The Ethiopian wolf is the world's rarest canid and Africa's most endangered carnivore. It exists in only seven isolated highland populations across Ethiopia, all above 3,000 metres. The total population is estimated at fewer than 500 individuals — less than the population of many villages.

It looks like a fox but is more closely related to the grey wolf, from which it diverged approximately 100,000 years ago. Its long legs and narrow muzzle are adaptations for hunting giant mole rats — the primary prey that makes up 96% of its diet — across open Afroalpine moorland.

The Bale Mountains hold the largest single population (approximately 300 wolves in the Web Valley and Sanetti Plateau). The Simien Mountains hold a much smaller population in the park's northern section.

### Where to See Them

**Bale Mountains — Web Valley:** The most reliable location in the world. The wolves are habituated to vehicles and pedestrians. At dawn, 3–5 wolves are typically visible hunting along the valley floor. Sawla Tours guides know individual animals by name.

**Simien Mountains:** Occasional sightings in the northern sections of the park, particularly near Ras Dashen. Far less reliable than Bale.

### Conservation Status

The Ethiopian wolf faces multiple threats: habitat loss (Afroalpine grassland conversion to agriculture), disease transmission from domestic dogs (rabies and distemper), and climate change pushing the Afroalpine zone higher and reducing habitat area. The Ethiopian Wolf Conservation Programme, based in Bale, runs vaccination campaigns for domestic dogs in surrounding villages.

# Gelada Baboon
## *Theropithecus gelada* · The Grass-Grazing Primate

**IUCN Status:** Least Concern  
**Population:** ~200,000 individuals  
**Endemic:** Yes — found only in Ethiopia  
**Best place to see:** Simien Mountains (Chenek, Sankaber), Guassa Community Conservation Area  
**Best time:** October–March (dry season, better visibility)

### Overview

The gelada is the world's only grass-grazing primate — a behavioural adaptation that sets it apart from every other monkey or ape on earth. While other primates supplement grass with fruit, seeds, and insects, the gelada lives almost entirely on grass blades and seeds. Its hands have evolved shortened fingers and a precision grip that allows it to harvest individual grass blades at extraordinary speed.

At dawn in the Simien Mountains, gelada herds of 300–800 individuals emerge from the cliff edges where they sleep, moving across the moorland in a slow, grazing mass. The social dynamics within large herds are complex — harems of 2–12 females and young are led by a single male, who must constantly defend his position against bachelor males lurking at the edges.

### The Red Chest Patch

The most distinctive feature of the gelada is the hourglass-shaped pink or red skin patch on the chest. In females, this patch changes colour to signal reproductive status. In males, it signals dominance and health. The patch gives the gelada its local Amharic name: "bleeding-heart baboon."

### Where to See Them

The Simien Mountains are the primary viewing location. The area around Chenek Camp (at 3,600m) consistently holds herds of 300–600 animals. The best time is early morning, when herds are moving from the cliff edges into the open moorland.

The Guassa Community Conservation Area, a 3-hour drive from Addis Ababa, offers excellent gelada viewing with far fewer tourists than the Simien Mountains.

# Walia Ibex
## *Capra walie* · The Cliff-Dwelling Wild Goat

**IUCN Status:** Endangered  
**Population:** ~1,000 individuals  
**Endemic:** Yes — found only in the Simien Mountains  
**Best place to see:** Simien Mountains (Chenek area)  
**Best time:** October–March

### Overview

The Walia ibex is found only on the rocky escarpments of the Simien Mountains — the only endemic large mammal restricted to a single Ethiopian national park. It is a member of the Capra genus (wild goats and ibex) and shares the spectacular recurved horns of its relatives, reaching 110cm in trophy males.

The ibex inhabits the cliff faces and rocky outcrops of the Simien escarpment at elevations between 2,500m and 4,500m. It is a master of vertical terrain — able to traverse cliff faces that appear vertical to the human eye, using rock ledges invisible to observers below.

### Conservation

The population has recovered from a low of approximately 150 individuals in the 1970s to around 1,000 today, following the establishment of the national park and anti-poaching measures. The primary ongoing threats are habitat loss through agricultural encroachment and competition with domestic livestock for grazing.

# Mountain Nyala
## *Tragelaphus buxtoni* · The Highland Antelope

**IUCN Status:** Endangered  
**Population:** ~2,500 individuals  
**Endemic:** Yes — found only in Ethiopia  
**Best place to see:** Bale Mountains National Park (Dinsho area)  
**Best time:** Year-round, mornings and evenings

### Overview

The mountain nyala is the largest of Ethiopia's endemic mammals — a spiral-horned antelope that inhabits the highland forests and heathland between 3,000m and 4,200m. It was the last large mammal to be described by science in Africa, not formally documented until 1908.

Males are spectacular: dark grey-brown with white spots and stripes, a mane running from head to shoulders, and massive lyre-shaped horns that can reach 120cm. Females are smaller, hornless, and reddish-brown.

The Bale Mountains hold the largest population. At Dinsho (the park headquarters), mountain nyala are habituated to human presence and visible at close range most mornings.

# Bale Monkey
## *Chlorocebus djamdjamensis* · The Bamboo Specialist

**IUCN Status:** Endangered  
**Population:** Estimated 5,000–10,000  
**Endemic:** Yes — found only in southern Ethiopian highlands  
**Best place to see:** Harenna Forest (Bale Mountains)  
**Best time:** Year-round

### Overview

The Bale monkey is a poorly-known endemic primate found only in the bamboo forests and montane woodland of southern Ethiopia. Unlike most primates, it has adapted to a diet dominated by bamboo shoots — a highly fibrous, nutritionally poor food source that most primates cannot process.

# Stark's Hare
## *Lepus starcki* · The Afroalpine Hare

**IUCN Status:** Least Concern  
**Endemic:** Yes — found only in the Ethiopian highlands  
**Best place to see:** Bale Mountains (Sanetti Plateau), Simien Mountains  
**Best time:** Year-round (most active at dawn and dusk)

### Overview

Stark's hare is a large hare found only in the Afroalpine moorland above 3,000m in Ethiopia. It is one of Ethiopia's least studied endemic mammals — its precise distribution and population size are poorly known. The Sanetti Plateau in the Bale Mountains offers the most reliable viewing.

# Ethiopian Hedgehog
## *Paraechinus aethiopicus* · The Desert Hedgehog

**IUCN Status:** Least Concern  
**Endemic to region:** Yes (Afar and Ogaden lowlands)  
**Best place to see:** Awash National Park, Danakil lowlands  
**Best time:** Nocturnal — best seen on night drives

### Overview

The Ethiopian hedgehog inhabits the arid and semi-arid lowlands of the Afar region and Ogaden. Smaller than the European hedgehog, it is adapted to extreme heat and can survive long periods without water. Nocturnal and secretive, it is rarely seen by casual visitors.

# Swayne's Hartebeest
## *Alcelaphus buselaphus swaynei* · The Ethiopian Hartebeest

**IUCN Status:** Endangered  
**Population:** ~3,500 individuals  
**Endemic:** Yes — subspecies found only in Ethiopia  
**Best place to see:** Senkele Wildlife Sanctuary (near Shashemene)  
**Best time:** Year-round

### Overview

Swayne's hartebeest is a subspecies of hartebeest found only in Ethiopia, once widespread across the southern grasslands but now reduced to a few protected areas. The Senkele Wildlife Sanctuary was established specifically to protect the remaining population. With its distinctively awkward-looking physique — sloping back, elongated face, and angular horns — the hartebeest is one of Africa's most recognisable antelopes.

# African Wild Ass
## *Equus africanus* · The Ancestor of the Domestic Donkey

**IUCN Status:** Critically Endangered  
**Population:** Fewer than 600 individuals globally  
**Best place to see:** Danakil Desert and Afar lowlands  
**Best time:** November–February

### Overview

The African wild ass is the ancestor of the domestic donkey and one of the world's most endangered large mammals. The Ethiopian subspecies (*E. a. africanus*) survives in the Danakil Desert and Afar lowlands in very small numbers. Ethiopia holds the most significant remaining population. Sightings are rare and extraordinary.

# Abyssinian Lion
## *Panthera leo* · The Ethiopian Lion

**IUCN Status:** Vulnerable (Ethiopian population: Critically Endangered)  
**Population in Ethiopia:** Fewer than 100 wild individuals estimated  
**Best place to see:** Gambella National Park, Mago National Park  
**Best time:** October–March (dry season)

### Overview

The Abyssinian lion — distinguished by its distinctive dark mane — once roamed across Ethiopia in large numbers. Today, wild lions survive only in a few remote southern and western parks. The Addis Ababa Zoo holds a population of this distinct subspecies, and the wild population in Gambella National Park may be the most significant remaining.

# Giant Mole Rat
## *Tachyoryctes macrocephalus* · The Wolf's Prey

**IUCN Status:** Vulnerable  
**Endemic:** Yes — found only in the Bale Mountains  
**Best place to see:** Sanetti Plateau and Web Valley, Bale Mountains  
**Best time:** Morning and late afternoon when active

### Overview

The giant mole rat is significant not for its own charisma but for its role as the primary prey of the Ethiopian wolf. On the Sanetti Plateau, giant mole rats are extraordinarily abundant — a single hectare may contain over 2,000 individuals. Their burrow systems cover the plateau surface, and the bobbing motion of their heads as they emerge to feed is a constant feature of the landscape.

Watching an Ethiopian wolf stalk, chase, and catch a giant mole rat is one of the most exciting small-scale predator-prey interactions in Africa.

# Guassa Gelada Population
## Community-Conserved Highland Plateau

**Location:** Guassa Community Conservation Area, North Shewa  
**Distance from Addis Ababa:** 3 hours  
**Best time:** Year-round  
**Crowds:** Minimal — far fewer visitors than Simien Mountains

### Overview

The Guassa plateau offers excellent gelada viewing in a community-conserved context — local villages manage the land and benefit directly from wildlife tourism. The landscape is classic Afroalpine moorland with dramatic rock outcrops, and the gelada herds here are less habituated to tourists than those in the Simien Mountains, making behaviour more natural and sightings more unpredictable (and more exciting).

Guassa also holds Ethiopian wolf, Menelik's bushbuck, and outstanding high-altitude birding.

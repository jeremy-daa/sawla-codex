# ETHIOPIAN WOLF

**File:** /content/species/ethiopian-wolf.md  
**Scientific name:** Canis simensis  
**Conservation status:** Endangered (IUCN)

## About the Ethiopian Wolf

The Ethiopian wolf is Africa's rarest wild canid and the world's most endangered wild dog species. With fewer than 500 individuals remaining in isolated highland populations, every sighting carries genuine weight.

They are not wolves in the North American or European sense. They are closer in build and ecology to a large-eared fox — long-legged, copper-red, with a distinctive white chest and pale underside. They hunt alone in Afroalpine grassland, pursuing giant mole-rats and other rodents. They are pack animals that split for solo hunts; territorial boundaries are maintained through howling and scent-marking that the observant visitor can witness.

The Ethiopian wolf exists in only six or seven isolated mountain populations. The Bale Mountains — specifically the Sanetti Plateau at above 4,000m — holds the largest single population: approximately 250 individuals.

## Where to See Them

**Bale Mountains National Park (primary):** The Sanetti Plateau is the most reliable site. Ethiopian wolves are active in the early morning and late afternoon. A properly guided visit of 2–3 days gives a high probability of sighting.

**Simien Mountains (secondary):** Smaller population, less predictable. Some sightings in higher sections near Ras Dashen.

**Other populations:** North Wollo, Arsi, Menz — small, remote, rarely visited by tourists.

## Best Viewing Conditions

- **Time of day:** 7:00–9:00am (hunt active) and 4:00–6:00pm (return to territory)
- **Season:** October to February (Sanetti Plateau accessible, clear days)
- **Guide:** A guide who knows individual wolves' territorial ranges dramatically improves success
- **Approach:** On foot, minimum 50m distance, no sudden movements

## Conservation Notes

Ethiopian wolves are threatened by habitat loss (encroachment from agriculture), disease (particularly rabies transmitted by domestic dogs), and genetic isolation between populations. The Ethiopian Wolf Conservation Programme (EWCP) leads vaccination and monitoring efforts. Tourism that funds Bale Mountain Lodge and employs local scouts directly supports conservation.

# GELADA MONKEY

**File:** /content/species/gelada-monkey.md  
**Scientific name:** Theropithecus gelada  
**Conservation status:** Vulnerable (IUCN)

## About the Gelada

The gelada is the world's only grass-grazing primate. While most primates are opportunistic feeders, geladas are specialists — around 90% of their diet is grass blades, stems, and seed heads, which they harvest with both hands simultaneously in a fluid bilateral plucking motion. Researchers estimate they repeat this motion up to 8,000 times per day.

They are not monkeys in the colloquial sense — they are their own ancient lineage, the last surviving species of a genus that once ranged across Africa. The characteristic red chest patch (more vivid on males, brighter still during estrus and social display) gives them the common name "bleeding heart monkey."

Geladas live in large troops with complex social structures. The basic unit is a "one-male unit": one dominant male, several females, and their young. Multiple one-male units aggregate into herds that can number into the hundreds — up to a thousand individuals on productive escarpments.

## Where to See Them

**Simien Mountains National Park:** The Chenek area is the most reliable site for large encounters. Troops sleep on cliff faces and climb to the plateau at dawn. Encounters at Sankaber are also possible.

**Guassa Community Conservation Area:** Less visited; smaller population but excellent conditions for observed encounters without park crowds.

## The Encounter

Geladas at Simien are habituated to human presence through years of contact with rangers, researchers, and travelers. This means they do not treat people as threats — you can sit quietly in the middle of a feeding troop without disturbing them.

This is not the same as domesticated. They are wild animals with active social hierarchies. Do not make direct eye contact with dominant males — sustained eye contact is a challenge display in gelada social language. Do not attempt to touch. Do not approach mothers with infants. Listen to your guide.

The encounter typically lasts as long as the troop remains in the area — sometimes an hour or more. The dominant male displays, the juveniles investigate your boots, the females continue feeding methodically. When they move to a new patch, you follow, or you don't, and they continue their morning without you.

# WALIA IBEX

**File:** /content/species/walia-ibex.md  
**Scientific name:** Capra walie  
**Conservation status:** Endangered (IUCN)

## About the Walia Ibex

The Walia ibex is a large wild goat found only in the rocky terrain of the Simien Mountains in northern Ethiopia. It is one of the rarest large mammals in Africa — population estimates range from 500 to 1,000 individuals, and the total range is restricted to a relatively small section of the Simien escarpment.

Males carry large, backward-curving horns that can reach 1 metre in length. They are remarkably sure-footed on near-vertical cliff faces, using ledges and outcrops that seem impossibly narrow. The sight of a fully-horned male standing motionless on a cliff edge against the Simien escarpment is one of the most dramatic wildlife images Ethiopia offers.

## Where to See Them

Best sightings are in the northern sections of the Simien Mountains National Park, particularly around Chenek and the rocky terrain above the escarpment edge. They are most active in the morning and late afternoon.

Unlike geladas, Walia ibex are not habituated — they treat people as a threat. Approach quietly, without sudden movement, and use binoculars or a long lens. Good sightings require patience.

# MOUNTAIN NYALA

**File:** /content/species/mountain-nyala.md  
**Scientific name:** Tragelaphus buxtoni  
**Conservation status:** Endangered (IUCN)

## About the Mountain Nyala

The mountain nyala is Ethiopia's largest endemic antelope — the only large antelope found exclusively in one country. Adult males stand up to 135cm at the shoulder and carry large, gently spiralling horns. Females are hornless and slightly smaller.

They live in the heather moorland, juniper forest, and subalpine grasslands of the Ethiopian highlands — primarily in the Bale Mountains and some highland areas of Arsi.

## Where to See Them

**Bale Mountains National Park:** Most reliably seen in the heather moorland between the park headquarters at Dinsho and the Sanetti Plateau. They are often visible from the road in the early morning, grazing in open areas adjacent to the forest edge.

Unlike the Ethiopian wolf, mountain nyala do not require an extended search. A morning drive through Bale in a good vehicle produces sightings on most days. Large bulls are occasionally solitary; females and young are typically seen in small groups.

# BALE MONKEY

**File:** /content/species/bale-monkey.md  
**Scientific name:** Chlorocebus djamdjamensis  
**Conservation status:** Vulnerable (IUCN)

## About the Bale Monkey

The Bale monkey (also called the Bale mountains vervet or grass monkey) is a specialist of Ethiopia's Harenna Forest — a montane cloud forest on the southern slopes of the Bale Mountains. It is found nowhere else.

Smaller and darker than other vervet species, the Bale monkey is primarily a bamboo feeder — consuming bamboo shoots, leaves, and young stems as its primary food source. This dietary specialisation ties it closely to the bamboo zones of the Harenna Forest.

## Where to See Them

The Harenna Forest is reached by road from the Sanetti Plateau (descending the southern escarpment) or from Dola Mena in the south. The bamboo zones within the forest are the best areas. A morning walk in the right bamboo section, with a guide who knows the territory, usually produces sightings.

# BLUE-WINGED GOOSE

**File:** /content/species/blue-winged-goose.md  
**Scientific name:** Cyanochen cyanoptera  
**Conservation status:** Vulnerable (IUCN)

## About the Blue-winged Goose

The blue-winged goose is a medium-sized goose endemic to the Ethiopian highlands, where it inhabits streams, lakes, and wetland meadows above approximately 2,000 metres. The distinctive blue-grey wing patch (visible in flight) gives the species its name.

It is often seen at wetland edges in the Ethiopian highlands — the Bale Mountains plateau lakes, the Sanetti Plateau streams, highland lakes in northern Ethiopia, and the marshes around Debre Berhan. Ethiopian birders consider it a foundation species for any highland bird list.

# STRESEMANN'S BUSHCROW

**File:** /content/species/stresemanns-bushcrow.md  
**Scientific name:** Zavattariornis stresemanni  
**Conservation status:** Endangered (IUCN)

## About Stresemann's Bushcrow

Stresemann's bushcrow is one of the world's strangest corvids — and one of the most range-restricted birds in Africa. It is found only in an area of approximately 6,000 square kilometres around Yabello in southern Ethiopia, and nowhere else on earth.

The bird is medium-sized with vivid blue wing feathers, an orange-tinged beak and face, a short crest, and the typically inquisitive corvid personality. It lives in small family groups in open Acacia and juniper woodland, feeding on insects and small vertebrates.

The restricted range is not fully explained by available habitat — suitable-looking habitat exists elsewhere in southern Ethiopia but the birds are absent. Climate modelling suggests the species may be on the edge of a thermal threshold.

## Where to See Them

**Yabello and surroundings:** The town of Yabello is the base. The best areas are the Acacia woodlands on the roadsides north and east of town. The birds are visible from vehicles and are not particularly wary.

**Arero Juniper Forest:** Secondary site, slightly higher altitude — good for a combined bushcrow + other southern endemic birding day.

Sawla Tours can incorporate Yabello into a southern Ethiopia cultural tour (Omo Valley–Yabello–Konso combination) or as part of a dedicated southern birding route.

# ROUGET'S RAIL

**File:** /content/species/rougets-rail.md  
**Scientific name:** Rougetius rougetii  
**Conservation status:** Least Concern (IUCN)

## About Rouget's Rail

Rouget's rail is a plump, rust-brown rail endemic to Ethiopia and Eritrea, found in highland wetlands, streamside vegetation, and marshy grasslands above approximately 2,000 metres. Despite being classified as Least Concern, it has a restricted range and is a target species for many birders visiting Ethiopia.

It is somewhat skulking in behaviour — more often heard (a repetitive, penetrating call) than seen — but can be observed walking at wetland edges at dawn and dusk.

**Where to see it:** Bale Mountains (Web Valley and plateau wetlands), Lake Tana marshes, and highland wetlands generally.

# SPOT-BREASTED LAPWING

**File:** /content/species/spot-breasted-lapwing.md  
**Scientific name:** Vanellus melanocephalus  
**Conservation status:** Vulnerable (IUCN)

## About the Spot-breasted Lapwing

The spot-breasted lapwing is a large, distinctive wader endemic to the Ethiopian highlands, where it inhabits Afroalpine grasslands and moorland above approximately 2,500 metres. The bold black head, white face, and spotted breast make it unmistakable.

**Where to see it:** Bale Mountains (Sanetti Plateau), Simien Mountains highlands, and Guassa Community Conservation Area. Usually encountered in small groups in open moorland.

# YELLOW-FRONTED PARROT

**File:** /content/species/yellow-fronted-parrot.md  
**Scientific name:** Poicephalus flavifrons  
**Conservation status:** Least Concern (IUCN)

## About the Yellow-fronted Parrot

The yellow-fronted parrot is a medium-sized parrot endemic to Ethiopia, found in forest and woodland habitats in the highlands and foothills. The distinctive yellow forehead patch (brighter in males) distinguishes it from other African parrots.

**Where to see it:** Forests of central and western Ethiopia — around Addis Ababa (Entoto Forest), Harenna Forest in Bale, and montane forests throughout the highlands. Often detected by its loud, characteristic call before being seen.

# PRINCE RUSPOLI'S TURACO

**File:** /content/species/ruspolis-turaco.md  
**Scientific name:** Tauraco ruspolii  
**Conservation status:** Vulnerable (IUCN)

## About Prince Ruspoli's Turaco

One of Ethiopia's rarest endemic birds and one of the most sought-after by specialist birders visiting Africa. The species was described in 1896 from a single specimen collected by Prince Eugenio Ruspoli — and its range and status remained poorly known for most of the 20th century.

It inhabits juniper-Podocarpus forest in a restricted area of south-central Ethiopia, principally around Mega, Shakiso, and Arero. The rich green plumage, white-tipped crest, and crimson primaries (visible in flight) make it a spectacular species to encounter.

**Where to see it:** Arero Forest and the Shakiso area in south Ethiopia. Requires a specialised birding route; Sawla Tours can incorporate into a combined southern Ethiopia birding circuit.

# MENELIK'S BUSHBUCK

**File:** /content/species/meneliks-bushbuck.md  
**Scientific name:** Tragelaphus scriptus meneliki  
**Conservation status:** Not separately assessed (subspecies)

## About Menelik's Bushbuck

Menelik's bushbuck is a subspecies of the common bushbuck — but a striking one, with darker, richer colouring than other forms and habitat restricted to Ethiopia's highland forests. It inhabits forest edges, riverine gallery forest, and montane woodland.

**Where to see it:** Bale Mountains (Dinsho headquarters area, Harenna Forest), Menagesha Suba Forest near Addis Ababa, and highland forests across central Ethiopia. Typically seen at forest edges in the early morning, sometimes in pairs.

# GIANT MOLE-RAT

**File:** /content/species/giant-mole-rat.md  
**Scientific name:** Tachyoryctes macrocephalus  
**Conservation status:** Vulnerable (IUCN)

## About the Giant Mole-Rat

The giant mole-rat is a large, burrowing rodent endemic to the Afroalpine grasslands of the Bale Mountains — specifically the Sanetti Plateau. It is the primary prey species of the Ethiopian wolf; the relationship between the two is one of the most specific predator-prey dynamics in African wildlife.

The mole-rat is large (up to 1.5kg), nearly blind, and spends most of its time underground, emerging to feed on roots and bulbs. At dawn on the Sanetti Plateau, they can be seen in burrow entrances — and Ethiopian wolves can be watched hunting them with the precision and patience of a cat staking a mouse hole.

For wildlife photographers, the wolf-mole-rat hunting sequence is one of the most photogenic behavioural opportunities in Ethiopia.

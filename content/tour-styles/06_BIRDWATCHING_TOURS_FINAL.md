## A Birding Destination Defined by Elevation

Ethiopia’s birding strength comes from geography and isolation. The highlands support localized species in grassland, cliffs, wetlands and forest. The Rift Valley creates lakes and dry corridors used by resident and migratory birds. The southeast opens into Somali–Masai habitats, while the southwest holds wetter forests and species with Central and West African affinities.

A single journey can move from thick-billed ravens and highland rails to bustards, coursers, hornbills, parrots, turacos, cranes, waterbirds and dry-country specialties. But the value is not only a long list. A well-designed tour gives enough time to understand habitat, voice, behavior and distribution—and to return when a difficult species does not appear on the first attempt.

Exact national totals, endemic counts and scientific names are deliberately not hard-coded throughout this page because taxonomic treatment changes. Sawla supplies a current target list with the assigned guide before departure.

## Signature Birding Experiences

### Begin on the central highland escarpment

Debre Libanos, the Jemma Valley and nearby highland sites can provide an excellent introduction to Ethiopian endemics, cliff birds, raptors and dry-valley species, depending on current access and the exact route.

### Bird the Awash–Ali Dege drylands

Search acacia and semi-arid habitats for bustards, coursers, sandgrouse, hornbills, starlings, weavers, raptors and localized dry-country species. Early starts are essential before heat builds.

### Explore the Rift Valley lakes

Lakes and wetlands around Ziway, Langano, Abijatta–Shalla, Hawassa and selected alternatives support waterbirds, raptors, woodland birds and migrants. Site choice depends on water levels, access and the wider itinerary.

### Cross Bale from Dinsho to the Sanetti Plateau

Bale is one of Ethiopia’s most productive birding regions. Forest edge, montane grassland, wetlands and Afroalpine habitat can produce Rouget’s rail, spot-breasted lapwing, blue-winged goose, thick-billed raven, wattled crane and many other highland birds, alongside Ethiopian wolf and mountain nyala.

### Descend into Harenna Forest

The southern escarpment and forest provide a dramatic habitat change. Time may be spent with forest birds, mixed flocks, canopy watching, night sounds and the ecological transition toward the lowlands.

### Search the Liben Plain

The grasslands near Negele are internationally important and require careful, low-impact birding. Access, grazing condition and current local guidance determine the field plan.

### Continue to Yabello and the southern drylands

Juniper, bushland and open country around Yabello can add Ethiopian bushcrow, white-tailed swallow and a strong set of southern specialties. This sector deserves repeated sessions rather than a hurried roadside stop.

### Add southwest forests

Bonga, Jimma-area sites, Bebeka and other southwest habitats can extend the list into moist forest and coffee landscapes. These routes require extra days and should be selected around specific targets.

## Best Time for Birdwatching in Ethiopia

### October to March

The strongest broad period for road access, comfortable field conditions in many regions and Palearctic migrants. Highland mornings can be cold; lowlands can still be very hot.

### April to May

Rain and local storms increase, but breeding activity, fresh plumage and green landscapes can reward flexible birders. Some roads become difficult.

### June to September

The highland rainy season transforms habitats and can produce breeding behavior and dramatic scenery. Travel is slower, some sites are muddy or inaccessible and sustained rain can reduce field time. Southern and eastern patterns differ from the north.

### Target-specific planning

The best date depends on whether the priority is migrants, breeding endemics, dry-country access, cranes, grassland condition, photography or a comprehensive list. Sawla plans around the targets rather than repeating one “best month” for every route.

## A Typical Day in the Field

Birding days often begin before sunrise with coffee or a packed breakfast. The first session targets vocal and active birds while light and temperature are favorable. A midday transfer or lodge break follows, then a late-afternoon field session.

Some days involve short roadside stops; others require several kilometers of walking on uneven ground or at altitude. The vehicle carries water, field lunches, scope, camera equipment and rain layers. The itinerary includes flexibility to revisit a site or respond to guide information.

Bird photographers need fewer sites, repeat sessions and better light rather than a maximum daily total. Sawla can reduce mileage, plan low-impact waiting, reserve more vehicle space and include charging, backups and packed meals.

Playback is conservative and only used when legal and appropriate. Nest disturbance, flushing and crowding are avoided. The guide’s welfare decision takes priority over an image.

## Responsible Birding

- Follow guide decisions on playback, approach and viewing time.
- Do not disturb nests, roosts or displaying birds.
- Remain on permitted tracks in fragile grasslands and wetlands.
- Keep groups small at sensitive sites.
- Avoid publishing precise locations of vulnerable nests or roosts.
- Use local guides and site fees where systems exist.
- Treat checklists as records, not guarantees.
- Update scientific names and endemic status before publication.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many endemic birds are there in Ethiopia?

The answer depends on taxonomy and whether species shared only with Eritrea are counted as national or regional endemics. Sawla avoids a static marketing number and provides a current target list using the taxonomic authority preferred by the group.

### What is the best first-time birding route?

A 14–18 day route through the central highlands, Awash, Rift Valley, Bale, Liben and Yabello offers the strongest balance of habitats and localized species. Shorter tours should focus on fewer regions.

### Can I combine birding with wildlife photography?

Yes. Bale is especially strong for Ethiopian wolf and mountain nyala, while Simien, Awash and the Rift Valley add other mammals. The itinerary should identify which sessions are bird-led and which are mammal-led.

### How difficult is the walking?

Most routes combine roadside and short-trail birding, but some targets require uneven ground, altitude or longer walks. Sawla adapts the field plan when mobility information is shared early.

### Do you arrange binocular permits?

Sawla can help process required documentation for binoculars and specialist optics before travel. Rules can change, so equipment details and serial numbers should be supplied well ahead of arrival.

### Can I bring a drone on a birding trip?

Only with required national approvals and local operating permission. Drones can disturb birds and may remain prohibited in protected or sensitive areas even when import permission exists.

### Will the guide use playback?

Playback is used selectively, legally and conservatively, never as a default. Sensitive, breeding, roosting and heavily pressured birds receive extra caution.

### Can a non-birding partner enjoy the trip?

Yes, with thoughtful design. Sawla can add wildlife, coffee, walking, cultural visits and comfortable lodge time while preserving essential birding hours.
